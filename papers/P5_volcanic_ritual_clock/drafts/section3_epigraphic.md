# Section 3: Epigraphic Evidence -- Pre-Indic Substrate in Old Javanese Inscriptions

**DRAFT v0.1 -- 2026-03-09**

If the slametan intervals are neither Hindu, Buddhist, nor Islamic in origin (Section 2), the question becomes whether an indigenous Javanese cosmological system existed prior to and independently of these imported traditions -- one capable of generating its own ritual logic. We turn to the epigraphic record for evidence.

Old Javanese inscriptions, predominantly royal charters (*prasasti*) recording land grants, boundary demarcations, and religious foundations, are the principal textual sources for pre-modern Javanese culture between the 8th and 15th centuries CE. These documents were composed in a milieu of extensive Indianisation: they employ the Saka calendar, Sanskrit liturgical vocabulary, and the conventions of Indian *dharmasastra*. Yet the question of whether indigenous cosmological concepts survived beneath this Indic surface -- and if so, in what form -- has received surprisingly little systematic quantitative treatment. We address this gap through a corpus-wide keyword analysis of the DHARMA ERC Nusantara epigraphy collection.

## 3.1 Corpus and method

The dataset comprises 268 inscriptions from the DHARMA ERC project (Griffiths et al. 2020--), a digital corpus of Southeast Asian epigraphy encoded in EpiDoc TEI-XML and released under a CC-BY 4.0 licence. The inscriptions span approximately the 5th to 15th centuries CE and represent the following language distribution: Old Javanese (*kawi*, n = 155, 58%), Sanskrit (n = 65, 24%), Old Sundanese (n = 14, 5%), Old Malay (n = 13, 5%), and unclassified or fragmentary texts (n = 21, 8%).

We constructed a keyword ontology of 25 ritual and cosmological terms, classifying each as *indic* (clear Sanskrit etymology and ritual function attested in Indian traditions), *pre-indic* (no plausible Sanskrit source; reconstructable to Proto-Malayo-Polynesian or attested as indigenous in comparative Austronesian linguistics), or *ambiguous* (Sanskrit lexeme potentially overlaying an indigenous concept). Classification followed standard comparative-linguistic criteria: a term was coded as pre-Indic only if it lacked a Sanskrit etymon and possessed cognates in at least one non-Indianised Austronesian language, or if its Proto-Malayo-Polynesian reconstruction was independently established in the comparative literature (Blust and Trussel 2010).

Each inscription was screened for the presence of these 25 keywords using automated text search against the full EpiDoc transcriptions, with manual verification for morphological variants and spelling alternations (e.g. *hyam* / *hyang*, *sapatha* / *sapatha*). We then computed frequency counts by keyword, by language, and by inscription, yielding both term-level prevalence and inscription-level pre-Indic ratios.

## 3.2 Results

Pre-Indic elements were detected in 126 of 268 inscriptions (47%). Across the full corpus, we identified 586 occurrences of Indic keywords, 165 occurrences of pre-Indic keywords, and 150 occurrences of ambiguous keywords. Four pre-Indic terms merit individual discussion.

***Hyam/hyang* (PMP \*qiang, 'spirit, divine').** This term appeared in 116 of 268 inscriptions (43%), making it the second most frequent ritual keyword in the entire corpus after the Saka calendar marker. The word is a reflex of Proto-Malayo-Polynesian \*qiang 'spirit, deity' (Blust and Trussel 2010), with cognates across the Austronesian family: Tagalog *anito*, Malay *yang* (as in *Yang Maha Esa*), Balinese *Sang Hyang Widi*. Its presence in the epigraphic record thus pre-dates Indianisation by definition, since its reconstructed ancestor belongs to a linguistic stratum several millennia older than the earliest Sanskrit loanwords in western Austronesia.

The distribution of *hyam/hyang* by inscription language is revealing. It appears in 102 of 155 Old Javanese inscriptions (66%), 6 of 14 Old Sundanese inscriptions (43%), 6 of 13 Old Malay inscriptions (46%), but in only 4 of 65 Sanskrit-only inscriptions (6%). The near-absence from Sanskrit texts, which were composed according to Indian literary conventions, contrasts sharply with its pervasive presence in texts composed in local languages. This asymmetry indicates that *hyam/hyang* was not borrowed from Sanskrit but rather persisted as an indigenous lexeme that Sanskrit-medium composition actively avoided while vernacular composition retained.

Crucially, the inscriptions that do contain *hyam/hyang* also employ Indic divine terminology -- *deva*, *devata*, *bhatara* -- in the same documents, often within a few lines of each other. The indigenous and imported terms co-exist rather than compete: *hyang* does not replace *deva*, nor does *deva* displace *hyang*. This pattern of co-existence, rather than substitution, is precisely what one would expect if an indigenous cosmological system had been overlaid, not replaced, by Indic imports.

***Manghuri* ('ancestor return').** This term appeared in 28 of 268 inscriptions (10%). It has no plausible Sanskrit etymon. In context, *manghuri* refers to the return or invocation of ancestral spirits in connection with land-grant ceremonies and boundary consecrations. The concept of ritually summoning ancestors to witness and sanction community decisions has broad Austronesian parallels: the Malagasy *famadihana* (bone-turning ceremony in which ancestors are physically reintroduced to the living community), the Torajan *ma'nene* (periodic cleaning and re-dressing of ancestral mummies), and the pan-Philippine practice of *pagdiwata* (ancestral spirit consultation). That the Old Javanese epigraphic record preserves a specific technical term for this practice strengthens the inference that ancestor-return rituals formed part of an indigenous ceremonial repertoire independent of Hindu or Buddhist ancestor veneration (*shraddha*, *pindadana*).

***Wuku* (210-day indigenous calendar).** The *wuku* or *pawukon* calendar -- a 30-week cycle of named seven-day weeks totalling 210 days -- appeared in 5 of 268 inscriptions (2%), a low absolute frequency that belies its significance. The *wuku* system has no Indian equivalent; it is structurally unrelated to the Saka solar calendar or the *tithi*-based lunar calendar, both of which are imported. In the DHARMA corpus, *wuku* dates co-occur with Saka dates in the same inscriptions, demonstrating that two independent calendrical systems operated simultaneously. Most notably, *wuku* appears in the Old Malay inscription Dharmasraya A from Sumatra (1286 CE), confirming that the indigenous calendar was not limited to Java but circulated across western Austronesian polities.

The survival of *wuku* alongside the dominant Saka system is analogous to the survival of *hyang* alongside *deva*: an indigenous institution persists beneath the Indic surface, neither fully absorbed nor fully displaced.

***Kabuyutan* (ancestral sacred site).** This term appears in the Old Sundanese corpus, notably in the Kebantenan inscriptions from West Java. A *kabuyutan* designates a site consecrated to ancestors -- literally 'that which pertains to the *buyut*' (ancestor, great-grandparent). The Batutulis inscription of Bogor combines *hyang* with *gunung* (mountain), linking indigenous divinity to mountain veneration in a formula that is distinctly non-Indic despite being inscribed during a period of deep Hindu-Buddhist influence. The Sundanese evidence thus corroborates the Javanese pattern: indigenous sacred-landscape concepts persisted in the epigraphic record as a detectable substrate.

## 3.3 Interpretation: the slametan belongs to the same indigenous substrate

The epigraphic data demonstrate that a layer of indigenous cosmology -- centred on ancestral spirits (*hyang*), ancestor-return rituals (*manghuri*), non-Indic time-reckoning (*wuku*), and sacred ancestral landscapes (*kabuyutan*) -- persisted throughout the period of Indianisation in Java, Sunda, and Sumatra. This substrate was not marginal: nearly half of all inscriptions in the corpus (47%) contain at least one pre-Indic element, and the flagship term *hyang* appears in two-thirds of all Old Javanese inscriptions.

The slametan death cycle does not appear in the prasasti. This absence is expected rather than problematic: the inscriptions are overwhelmingly concerned with royal administration, land tenure, and temple foundations, not with the domestic mortuary practices of commoners. The slametan, as Geertz (1960) and Koentjaraningrat (1994) describe it, is a village-level, household-organised ritual -- precisely the kind of practice that would be transmitted orally rather than engraved in stone. But the epigraphic record does establish three facts critical to situating the slametan within a broader cultural stratigraphy.

First, it confirms that an indigenous cosmological vocabulary existed and was actively used in formal, literate contexts, not merely in folk tradition. If *hyang* could survive in royal charters composed by Sanskrit-trained scribes, the persistence of indigenous mortuary intervals in oral village practice is *a fortiori* plausible.

Second, it demonstrates the mechanism of cultural layering: Indic and indigenous systems co-existed, with the former providing official liturgical language and the latter providing the operative cosmological categories. The slametan underwent an identical process: its temporal structure remained indigenous while its verbal content was successively re-clothed in Hindu, then Islamic, terms (Aizid 2015; Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020).

Third, the cross-regional distribution of pre-Indic elements -- *hyang* in Java, Sunda, and Sumatra; *wuku* in Java and Sumatra; *kabuyutan* in Sunda -- indicates that the indigenous substrate was not a localised Javanese peculiarity but a shared western Austronesian heritage. This is consistent with the pan-Austronesian mortuary structure documented in Section 4, and it strengthens the inference that the slametan intervals derive from a deep indigenous layer rather than from any single imported tradition.

In sum, the epigraphy does not record the slametan directly, but it preserves the cosmological soil in which the slametan grew. The indigenous system of ancestral spirits, spirit-return, and non-Indic time-reckoning that is legible in the prasasti is the same system that, at the village level, generated the 3--7--40--100--1000 day mortuary calendar. The next section asks whether this system is uniquely Javanese in structure or whether it reflects a broader Austronesian pattern -- and, if the latter, what accounts for the variation in specific numerical intervals across different Austronesian communities.
