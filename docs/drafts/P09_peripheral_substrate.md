## Abstract (placeholder)

Geographic and cultural peripheries in Nusantara — Bali Aga villages, 
Tegal-Banyumas dialect zones, Toraja highlands, Muna island — preserve 
archaic layers that have been overwritten in historical centres by 
successive waves of Indianization, Islamization, and colonial contact. 
We propose that this *peripheral conservatism* constitutes an 
archaeologically legible substrate recoverable through three independent 
channels: (1) formal register vocabulary (Krama/Alus), (2) mortuary 
ritual structure, and (3) botanical burial practice. Cross-referencing 
these channels against the Malagasy record — a time-capsule of 
~1200 CE Nusantaran culture isolated before Islamization — allows 
triangulation of pre-Hindu cultural elements with dateable lower bounds. 
Computational screening of 268 inscriptions (DHARMA), 137 cultures 
(Pulotu), and lexical databases (ABVD) provides a pilot dataset for 
this framework.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Periphery as Archive

Standard models of cultural diffusion assume that innovations originate 
at centres and spread outward. A corollary, less often stated, is that 
*centres overwrite themselves* — the same prestige pressure that 
disseminates innovations also erases the strata beneath them.

In Nusantara, three successive overwriting events have shaped the 
observable cultural record:

```
~400–1500 CE  : Indianization (Sanskrit, Hindu-Buddhist cosmology,
                cremation, caste-inflected speech registers)
~1200–1700 CE : Islamization (burial, prayer, dietary law,
                Arabic loanwords into formal registers)  
~1600–1945 CE : Colonialism (Dutch administrative vocabulary,
                Christian burial in some regions, 
                standardization of written Malay/Indonesian)
```

Communities that escaped the full force of these waves — by geography, 
resistance, or isolation — constitute involuntary archives. We identify 
four primary peripheral zones for this analysis:

1. **Bali Aga** (Trunyan, Tenganan): pre-Majapahit Balinese, retreated 
   to highlands when Majapahit colonized lowland Bali
2. **Banyumas-Tegal corridor**: Javanese dialect zone that never became 
   a centre of Mataram power; preserves older Krama forms
3. **Toraja highlands, Sulawesi**: highland community with intact 
   mortuary ritual; linguistically relevant to Paper 8 substrate work
4. **Muna island, Sulawesi**: Paper 8 primary focus; oldest confirmed 
   cave art context (≥67,800 BP via Wallace's Line argument)

The fifth — and most powerful — peripheral archive is **Madagascar**. 
Austronesian settlers arrived ~1200 CE, carrying a snapshot of 
Nusantaran culture *after* Indianization but *before* Islamization. 
Any cultural element shared between Madagascar and the above peripheral 
zones, but absent from Islamized Javanese/Malay centres, is a candidate 
pre-Islamic (and possibly pre-Hindu) substrate element.

## 2. Three Channels of Substrate Recovery

### 2.1 Channel 1: Formal Register as Linguistic Fossil

**The Krama paradox**

Javanese maintains a strict register system:
- *Ngoko* (low): everyday speech, changes rapidly
- *Madya* (mid): intermediate, urban educated
- *Krama Inggil* (high): ritual, court, elder-address

Standard assumption: Krama is a Hindu-era innovation, 
borrowed from Sanskrit-inflected court language.

**Counter-observation (fieldwork, this study):**

Bali *Basa Alus* (high register) shares vocabulary with Javanese 
Krama Inggil that cannot be explained by post-Majapahit borrowing alone — 
the two languages diverged *before* the relevant borrowing window. 
Similarly, Tegal-area Krama preserves forms that Solo/Yogyakarta 
Krama has smoothed over through centuries of standardization.

**Hypothesis: Peripheral Krama Conservation (PKC)**

> Formal registers in peripheral zones preserve pre-Sanskrit vocabulary 
> *fossilized* in ritual contexts, because:
> (a) ritual language changes more slowly than everyday language
> (b) peripheral communities had less prestige pressure to 
>     adopt Sanskrit-derived forms
> (c) the formal register was the last to be replaced during Islamization

**Testable prediction:** Krama vocabulary in Tegal/Banyumas and 
Bali Alus should show higher overlap with reconstructed 
Proto-Malayo-Polynesian (PMP) forms than Solo-standard Krama, 
when controlling for Sanskrit borrowings.

**Method:** KawiKupas (ModernKataKupas adapted for Kawi/Old Javanese) 
applied to:
- Van den Berg (1996) Muna lexicon
- ABVD Bali Aga dialect entries
- Tegal/Banyumas dialect wordlists (to be compiled)
- Malagasy formal register (Basa Ofisialy)

### 2.2 Channel 2: Mortuary Ritual Structure

**The Trunyan case**

Bali Aga of Trunyan practice *mepasah*: exposed above-ground burial, 
body placed under Taru Menyan tree, decomposition by natural processes, 
bones later collected and arranged at site periphery.

Key structural elements:
```
1. No inhumation (not buried)
2. No cremation (not burned)  
3. Botanical neutralization of decomposition odor
4. Tripartite cemetery classification by death-type
5. Women excluded from burial procession
6. Volcanic context: site adjacent to active Mt. Batur
7. Belief: improper burial → volcanic eruption
```

Element 7 is remarkable: **the community explicitly links mortuary 
practice to volcanic activity**. This is not metaphorical — Mt. Batur 
is active and visible from the cemetery. Whether the causation runs 
from volcano to ritual (volcanic eruption prompted ritual elaboration) 
or ritual to volcano (ritual designed to appease volcanic forces) is 
undeterminable from ethnography alone, but the association is robust.

**Cross-regional parallels:**

| Element | Trunyan (Bali Aga) | Toraja (Sulawesi) | Dayak (Kalimantan) | Malagasy |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Above-ground/exposed phase | ✓ (mepasah) | ✓ (tau-tau effigy period) | ✓ (some groups) | ✓ (famadihana) |
| Bone collection/secondary | ✓ | ✓ (ma'nene) | ✓ | ✓ (famadihana) |
| Aromatic botanical element | ✓ (Taru Menyan) | ? | ? | ✓ (specific shroud plants) |
| Tripartite death classification | ✓ | partial | ? | ? |
| Volcanic association in belief | ✓ (explicit) | indirect | absent | absent |

**Note:** Malagasy famadihana (bone-turning ritual, ~7-year cycle) and 
Toraja ma'nene (annual bone-cleaning) are structurally homologous — 
both involve secondary treatment of remains, both have strong community 
participation requirements, both involve *textile wrapping* of bones. 
This has been noted before but never systematically quantified.

### 2.3 Channel 3: Botanical Burial Complex

**Observation (fieldwork, this study):**

Kamboja (*Plumeria* spp.) is ubiquitous at Javanese Muslim cemeteries. 
Informants consistently explain its presence as odor-neutralization. 
This is partially correct — *Plumeria* produces iridoid compounds with 
mild antimicrobial properties — but the explanation is functionally 
identical to the Trunyan explanation for Taru Menyan.

**Hypothesis: Botanical Substitution Chain**

```
Pre-Hindu Nusantara:
  → Exposed burial + site-specific aromatic tree
  → (Taru Menyan at Trunyan = surviving example)

Indianization/burial normalization:
  → Inhumation becomes dominant
  → Aromatic botanical retained but function shifts
  → From "decomposition neutralizer" to "grave marker"

Islamization:
  → Inhumation reinforced
  → Botanical element survives as "tradition" (not "ritual")
  → Kamboja = portable substitute for non-portable Taru Menyan

Result: Islamic Javanese cemetery with kamboja = 
        three-layer palimpsest of burial practice
```

**Prediction:** Kamboja or functionally equivalent aromatic species 
should appear in Malagasy burial contexts if this represents 
pre-Islamic Nusantaran practice exported ~1200 CE.

**Testable via:** Ethnobotanical literature on Malagasy burial plants 
+ Pulotu database botanical annotations (where available).

## 3. The 1000-Day Synthesis (H-TOM Connection)

[Cross-reference Paper 5 findings from 2026-03-06]

The three channels above converge on a single mechanism when considered 
alongside the 1000-day slametan finding:

```
VOLCANIC SOIL (pH 4.5–5.5)
         ↓
Fast organic decomposition (~1000 days to "tanah")
         ↓
    Two consequences:
    
    A. RITUAL CLOCK (Channel 2 + Paper 5)
       → Slametan 1000-hari calibrated to 
         volcanic soil decomposition rate
       → Ritual calendar IS a taphonomic calendar
       
    B. ARCHAEOLOGICAL INVISIBILITY (Papers 1, 7)
       → Same soil chemistry that sets ritual clock
       → Destroys organic archaeological evidence
       → Explains absence of wooden structures,
         organic materials, non-stone burials
```

This means: the absence of pre-Hindu burial evidence in Javanese 
archaeological record is **doubly explained** —

1. Taphonomic: volcanic burial (Papers 1, 7)
2. Material: exposed burial = no archaeological signature 
   (no pit, no grave goods in fixed location, bones scattered)

An exposed burial practice on volcanic plains would leave 
*essentially zero* archaeological trace after 1000+ years. 
This is not a problem for the hypothesis — it IS the hypothesis.

## 4. The Peripheral Conservatism Framework (PCF)

**Core proposition:**

> The most archaic Nusantaran cultural layers are recoverable 
> not from historical centres but from peripheral zones that 
> resisted successive overwriting events. Three types of 
> periphery are archaeologically distinct:
>
> Type A — Geographic: islands, highlands, crater lakes 
>           (Trunyan/Batur, Muna, Toraja)
> Type B — Political: zones never incorporated into prestige 
>           centres (Tegal-Banyumas, Banyuwangi)  
> Type C — Temporal: communities exported before overwriting 
>           events (Madagascar ~1200 CE)

**The Malagasy test:**

Madagascar is unique because it is a Type C periphery with a 
*known departure date* (~1200 CE). Any substrate element present in 
Madagascar must predate ~1200 CE. If the same element appears in 
Type A or B peripheries, it likely predates the relevant overwriting 
event in those regions too.

This gives us a dateable lower bound for substrate elements without 
requiring radiocarbon dates on intangible cultural material.

## 6. Significance

This paper proposes a methodological inversion: instead of looking for 
*the oldest evidence at the centre*, look for *the most conservative 
practice at the periphery*. This is particularly powerful for Nusantara 
because:

1. Volcanic taphonomy (Papers 1, 7) ensures centres have the 
   *least* physical evidence
2. Successive cultural overwriting ensures centres have the 
   *least* intangible substrate
3. Peripheral zones have survived both processes better

The implication extends beyond archaeology: **the living communities 
of Trunyan, Tegal, Toraja, and Muna are not cultural relics — 
they are active archives of a Nusantaran deep past that volcanic 
burial has erased from the material record.**

## 5. East Java as Multiple Substrate Contact Zone

### 5.1 The Linguistic Zoo Problem

East Java presents a phenomenon that appears paradoxical at first:
four mutually unintelligible languages spoken by populations that
are **physically indistinguishable** from one another.

```
In one room: Javanese, Madurese, Osing, Balinese speaker
→ Visual identification: impossible
→ Auditory identification (once they speak): immediate

This is not unusual — it is the global norm.
The paradox dissolves once we separate two independent channels:

GENETICS (physical appearance):
→ Records: who reproduced with whom
→ Timeframe: recent millennia
→ East Java result: extensive intermarriage
  → genetic convergence → similar phenotype

LANGUAGE:
→ Records: community identity and origin
→ Can be maintained WITHOUT genetic isolation
→ East Java result: distinct origins maintained
  as identity even after genetic mixing
```

**Comparative case — East Asia:**

Japanese, Korean, and Mandarin Chinese are not linguistically
related — they belong to entirely separate language families
with zero demonstrable common ancestry. Yet speakers are
physically similar (shared East Asian phenotype from population
expansion ~40,000 BP). Physical similarity reflects recent
genetic history; linguistic distinctness reflects much deeper
and more complex population histories.

East Java is the Nusantaran equivalent of this phenomenon.

### 5.2 The Four Players and Their Origins

```
JAVANESE (dataran rendah Jawa Timur):
Family:  Austronesian → Javanic branch
Origin:  Wave dari barat, Taiwan → Philippines
         → Sulawesi → Java ~3,500-4,000 BP
Landscape: Volcanic plains — VCS applies fully

MADURESE (Pulau Madura + coastal Jawa Timur):
Family:  Austronesian → DISTINCT branch from Javanese
         (diverged BEFORE Javanese-Malagasy split)
Origin:  Likely wave dari timur — Lesser Sunda
         direction, NOT from mainland Java
Critical: Pulau Madura = LIMESTONE, not volcanic
         → Different geological substrate
         → Different adaptive pressures
         → Different material culture
         → Maintained distinct identity across Selat Madura

BALINESE (Banyuwangi corridor + west Bali):
Family:  Austronesian → Bali-Sasak-Sumbawa branch
         (linguistically closer to Lombok than to Java)
Origin:  From east — Lombok/Sumbawa direction
Note:    Cultural Javanization (Majapahit) ≠
         linguistic Javanization
         Bali culturally = Java; linguistically ≠ Java

OSING (Banyuwangi):
Family:  Javanic — but ARCHAIC branch
Status:  Most linguistically interesting of all four
Origin:  Remnant of Blambangan kingdom — last Hindu
         kingdom on Java, survived until ~1770s
         (while all other Java had Islamized)
Why distinct: 
  Blambangan = extreme eastern periphery
             + Hindu identity maintenance
             + contact with Bali
             = triple isolation mechanism
Prediction: Osing preserves pre-Islamic Javanese
            better than any other Javanese dialect
            → Directly testable with KawiKupas
            → High-priority target for Paper 8 substrate work

BONUS — TENGGER (lereng Bromo-Tengger):
Family:  Javanic — highly archaic
Status:  Only Hindu community on mainland Java
         that did not Islamize
Location: INSIDE Tengger caldera rim
         = Type A geographic peripheral (Paper 9 §4)
Significance:
  Linguistic archaism + religious archaism
  + volcanic landscape isolation
  = TRIPLE FOSSIL
  Most relevant community for Paper 11 VCS hypothesis
```

### 5.3 Why They Did Not Merge

Contact between these communities was intense — trade, warfare,
intermarriage all occurred over millennia. Yet languages remained
distinct. Three mechanisms explain this:

**Mechanism 1: Physical barrier (Selat Madura)**
Sufficient to slow convergence; insufficient to prevent contact.
Result: Madurese has Javanese loanwords but distinct core grammar.
Exactly the pattern expected from contact-with-barrier.

**Mechanism 2: Geological identity (Limestone vs Volcanic)**
Madura's limestone geology creates materially different lifeways
from volcanic Java. Different agriculture, different building
materials, different hazard landscape. Material distinctness
reinforces identity distinctness reinforces linguistic distinctness.
This is the VCS argument in reverse: Madura was NOT volcanically
selected → different cultural toolkit → maintained separation.

**Mechanism 3: Active identity maintenance**
Language is not merely communication — it is identity declaration.
Communities in contact CHOOSE whether to converge or maintain
distinctness. East Java communities maintained distinctness
deliberately, across centuries of intense contact.

### 5.4 Critical Implication for Substrate Recovery

```
Standard assumption in substrate work:
"Find ONE pre-Austronesian substrate for Java"

Revised assumption (from East Java evidence):
"There were MULTIPLE pre-Austronesian populations
 in East Java, corresponding to different
 geological zones and arrival vectors"

This means:

Volcanic plains substrate ≠ Limestone Madura substrate
Tengger caldera substrate ≠ Banyuwangi corridor substrate

Paper 8 substrate recovery (Sulawesi, 6 languages):
→ Similarly, do not assume ONE Sulawesi substrate
→ Different islands, different geological contexts
   = potentially different pre-Austronesian populations

Malagasy as clean reference:
→ Departed from ONE source population (~1200 CE)
→ Relatively uniform substrate
→ Use as baseline, then compare with
  the MORE COMPLEX East Java multi-substrate picture
```
