# Selametan 7-40-100-1000 Day Cycle: Source Analysis

## Question
Is the Javanese selametan mortuary cycle (3-7-40-100-1000 days after death) documented in any Hindu, Buddhist, or Islamic text?

## Findings

### Hindu Texts: NO MATCH
- Hindu shraddha follows 10-13 day cycle (sapindikarana on day 12-13)
- Annual observances during Pitru Paksha
- No 40-day, 100-day, or 1000-day markers in Hindu scripture

### Buddhist Texts: PARTIAL OVERLAP
- 7-day unit exists: prayers every 7 days for 7 weeks = 49 days (bardo/intermediate state)
- 100-day mourning appears in East Asian Buddhism but may derive from Confucian influence
- NO 40-day or 1000-day interval in any Buddhist text
- Buddhist sequence: 7-49-100-(1 year)-(3 years)

### Islamic Texts: NO BASIS
- Orthodox Islamic mourning limited to 3 days (widows: 4 months 10 days)
- 3rd, 7th, 40th, 100th, 1000th day commemorations explicitly called *bid'ah* (innovation)
- Recognized by Islamic scholars as local cultural customs, not Islamic doctrine

### VERDICT: The complete 3-7-40-100-1000 sequence is UNIQUELY JAVANESE
No single religious tradition contains this unified system. It is a Javanese synthesis that borrows elements from multiple sources while adding its own logic.

## Geertz 1960 Confirmation
Chapter 6 of *The Religion of Java* ("The Slametan Cycles: Death") documents:
- Slametans at 3, 7, 40, 100 days, then 1st and 2nd anniversaries, finally 1000 days
- Each slametan increases in size (guests + chant length)
- **KEY DETAIL: 1000 days = believed to be when the body has completely decayed to dust**
- This links the ritual calendar directly to physical decomposition (taphonomic process!)

## Kitab Primbon
- Javanese compendium of esoteric knowledge (divination, numerology, ritual procedures)
- Most famous: Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna (337 chapters)
- Contains *tatacara selamatan* (procedures for ceremonial meals including death commemorations)
- Contains pawukon (210-day wuku cycle) — confirms indigenous calendar system
- Available on Javanese Wikisource: jv.wikisource.org/wiki/Kitab_Primbon_Betaljemur_Adammakna
- British Library: "Javanese compendia of religious knowledge" blending Islamic, Hindu-Buddhist, indigenous elements

## Cross-Austronesian Comparison

### Madagascar (Famadihana)
- Different numbers: 3-5-7 year cycles (not fixed post-death day schedule)
- Timing determined by mpanandro (astrological diviner) + ancestor dreams
- BUT structurally analogous: death as gradual process, multiple interventions
- Key source: "Austronesian Mortuary Ritual in History" (Ethnohistory, Duke UP)

### Toraja (Sulawesi)
- Death explicitly a gradual process toward Puya (land of souls)
- Body remains in house weeks to years ("sick", not "dead")
- Ma'nene (exhumation/re-dressing) annually or every 1-3 years
- No fixed post-death day intervals, but same multi-stage principle

### Bali
- Ngaben (cremation) delayed weeks to years
- Post-cremation: ashes scattered ~12 days after
- More focused on ritual purity + calendar than fixed-day intervals

### Philippines (pre-colonial)
- Secondary burial (bogwa = bone cleaning/rewrapping) documented
- Contemporary 9-day + 40-day likely Spanish Catholic overlay
- Pre-colonial intervals not well-documented

### Pan-Austronesian Pattern
The consistent feature is NOT the specific numbers but the STRUCTURE:
1. Death as gradual transition, not single event
2. Multiple ritual interventions over months to years
3. Final ritual marking complete separation of deceased from living
4. Connection between body decomposition and soul journey
5. Secondary treatment of remains (cleaning, exhumation, rewrapping, scattering)

## Implications for P5

### The H-TOM Connection
Geertz reports that 1000 days = body fully decayed to dust. This means:
- The selametan cycle is calibrated to TAPHONOMIC PROCESSES
- The ritual calendar maps directly to physical decomposition rates
- In volcanic landscapes, sedimentation buries remains faster → potentially disrupting the ritual cycle
- This connects P5 (cosmological stratigraphy) to P1/P2 (volcanic taphonomy) and P9 (burial rates)

### Testable Hypothesis
If the 7-40-100-1000 pattern is pre-Indic Javanese:
1. It should NOT appear in Hindu/Buddhist/Islamic texts → CONFIRMED
2. Structurally similar patterns should exist in other Austronesian cultures → CONFIRMED (gradual death concept)
3. The specific numbers may reflect local environmental observation (decomposition rates)
4. The Primbon (which preserves indigenous knowledge systems) should contain it → CONFIRMED

## Javanese Names for Death Slametan Intervals
(Source: Koentjaraningrat, via Moussons journal openedition.org/moussons/4302)

1. **Sedhekah nigang ndinteni** — 3rd day
2. **Mitung ndinteni** — 7th day
3. **Ngawangdasa dinten** — 40th day
4. **Sedhekah nyatus** — 100th day
5. **Sedhekah mendhak sepisan** — 1st anniversary
6. **Sedhekah mendhak kaping kalih** — 2nd anniversary
7. **Sedhekah nyewu** — 1000th day

### Soul Journey (Ki Sabdalangit)
"Around the 40th day, the spirit begins to leave the house to go to the eternal world...
from 100 days he goes even further. It is from 1000 days that the deceased enters the eternal dimension."

### Robert Hertz Connection
The Moussons article explicitly links "corpse decay" and "ritual steps up to the 1000-days peak" to a process toward "perfection" — citing Robert Hertz's theory that physical decomposition parallels social/spiritual transition of the deceased. This is the theoretical foundation connecting P5 to H-TOM.
