## Abstract

Geographic and cultural peripheries in Nusantara — Bali Aga villages,
Toraja highlands, Muna island — preserve archaic cultural layers that
successive waves of Indianization, Islamization, and colonialism have
overwritten in historical centres. We propose the *Peripheral Conservatism
Framework* (PCF), which identifies three types of peripheral archive
(geographic, political, temporal) and demonstrates their utility for
substrate recovery through computational analysis of three independent
channels: lexical conservation, mortuary ritual structure, and botanical
burial practice. Madagascar serves as a Type C (temporal) periphery with a
known departure date (~1200 CE), providing dateable lower bounds for
substrate elements without requiring radiocarbon dating of intangible
cultural material.

Analysis of 210 basic vocabulary concepts from the Austronesian Basic
Vocabulary Database (ABVD) reveals that Balinese retains 40.3%
Proto-Malayo-Polynesian (PMP) cognates compared to 33.0% for standard
Javanese — a peripheral advantage concentrated in nature-domain vocabulary
(72.7% vs 45.5%). Merina Malagasy (40.8%) provides a mid-point between
Old Javanese (55.3%) and modern Javanese, consistent with ~1200 CE
departure. A critical correction to botanical analyses is established:
*Plumeria* (kamboja), ubiquitous at Javanese cemeteries, is a New World
species introduced in the 1560s. The genuine pan-Austronesian aromatic
burial candidate is *Canarium* spp. (dammar/ramy), present as ceremonial
incense in both Indonesia and Madagascar.

Screening of 268 Old Javanese inscriptions (DHARMA corpus) confirms that
the described civilization was overwhelmingly organic (63.4% of inscriptions
mention organic materials vs 27.2% lithic), explaining the archaeological
"blank" as preservation bias rather than cultural absence. The Indianization
itself proves non-permanent: Sanskrit vocabulary ratio declines from 0.807
(9th century) to 0.569 (13th century), while pre-Indic terminology
diversifies. Together, these findings support the PCF as a systematic
methodology for recovering invisible pre-Hindu Nusantaran cultural
substrates from living peripheral communities.

## 1. Introduction: The Periphery as Archive

Standard models of cultural diffusion assume that innovations originate
at centres and spread outward. A corollary, less often stated, is that
*centres overwrite themselves* — the same prestige pressure that
disseminates innovations also erases the strata beneath them.

In Nusantara, three successive overwriting events have shaped the
observable cultural record:

- ~400–1500 CE: Indianization (Sanskrit, Hindu-Buddhist cosmology,
  cremation, caste-inflected speech registers)
- ~1200–1700 CE: Islamization (inhumation burial, prayer, dietary law,
  Arabic loanwords into formal registers)
- ~1600–1945 CE: Colonialism (Dutch administrative vocabulary,
  Christian burial in some regions, standardization of written
  Malay/Indonesian)

Communities that escaped the full force of these waves — by geography,
resistance, or isolation — constitute involuntary archives. In volcanic
Java, this archival function acquires special significance: the same
volcanic processes that bury physical archaeological evidence at
2.4–6.2 mm/year (Amien 2026a) also accelerate the decomposition of
organic materials that constituted the majority of pre-Hindu material
culture (see §5).

This paper proposes the *Peripheral Conservatism Framework* (PCF):
a systematic methodology for recovering pre-Hindu Nusantaran cultural
substrates not from archaeological excavation at overwritten centres,
but from computational analysis of living peripheral communities. We
demonstrate the framework through three independent recovery channels
— lexical, ritual, and botanical — using Madagascar as a dateable
calibration point.

### 1.1 Peripheral Zones Analyzed

We identify four primary peripheral zones plus one temporal archive:

1. **Bali Aga** (Trunyan, Tenganan): pre-Majapahit Balinese communities,
   retreated to highlands when Majapahit colonized lowland Bali (~14th c.)
2. **Toraja highlands, Sulawesi**: highland community with intact
   mortuary ritual; linguistically distinct from coastal Bugis/Makassar
3. **Muna island, Sulawesi**: oldest confirmed cave art context in the
   region (Oktaviana et al. 2024); linguistically distinct from mainland
4. **Tengger caldera, East Java**: only Hindu community on mainland Java
   that did not Islamize; volcanic landscape isolate
5. **Madagascar** (Type C — temporal): Austronesian settlers arrived
   ~1200 CE, carrying a snapshot of Nusantaran culture *after*
   Indianization but *before* Islamization

## 2. The Peripheral Conservatism Framework (PCF)

### 2.1 Three Types of Periphery

> The most archaic Nusantaran cultural layers are recoverable not from
> historical centres but from peripheral zones that resisted successive
> overwriting events. Three types of periphery are archaeologically
> distinct:

**Type A — Geographic:** Islands, highlands, crater lakes. Physical
isolation slows cultural diffusion. Examples: Trunyan (Batur caldera),
Muna (island), Toraja (highlands), Tengger (caldera rim).

**Type B — Political:** Zones never incorporated into prestige centres.
No court standardization pressure. Examples: Tegal-Banyumas corridor
(never a Mataram power centre), Banyuwangi/Osing (Blambangan: last Hindu
polity on Java, fell ~1770s).

**Type C — Temporal:** Communities exported before an overwriting event.
The departure date provides a terminus ante quem for substrate elements.
Example: Madagascar (~1200 CE departure = post-Indianization,
pre-Islamization snapshot).

### 2.2 The Malagasy Test

Madagascar is the most powerful element of the PCF because it is a
Type C periphery with a *known departure date*. Any cultural element
present in Madagascar must predate ~1200 CE. If the same element appears
in Type A or B peripheries in Indonesia, it likely predates the relevant
overwriting event in those regions too.

This gives dateable lower bounds for intangible substrate elements
without requiring radiocarbon dates on immaterial cultural practices.

### 2.3 The Scale Paradox

Not all peripheries conserve equally. E043 (this study) reveals that
Tengger — a Type A geographic peripheral inside the Bromo caldera —
actually shows *lower* PMP cognacy than standard Javanese (27.7% vs
33.0%, McNemar p=0.015). Meanwhile, Balinese — a much larger peripheral
community — shows *higher* PMP cognacy (40.3%, borderline significant).

This suggests a **scale-dependent conservation** principle: peripheral
conservatism requires a critical mass of speakers. Small, extreme
isolates (Tengger: ~100,000 speakers in a caldera) undergo
innovation-by-drift, while larger peripheral communities (Bali: ~3.3
million speakers; Madagascar: ~25 million) maintain conservative
patterns through internal standardization pressure.

PCF therefore applies most reliably to:
- **Large** geographic peripheries (Bali, Madagascar)
- **Political** peripheries (Banyumas: millions of speakers)
- **Temporal** peripheries (Madagascar: frozen at departure)

And less reliably to:
- **Tiny** geographic isolates (Tengger, possibly Trunyan village)

This distinction has practical implications for future substrate
recovery work: prioritize Bali Alus and Malagasy formal registers over
Tengger ritual vocabulary for archaic lexical recovery.

## 3. Linguistic Conservation at the Periphery

### 3.1 The Cognacy Gradient (E043)

To test whether peripheral varieties preserve more proto-language
vocabulary, we extracted forms for 8 language varieties from the ABVD
CLDF dataset (Greenhill et al. 2008; 210 basic vocabulary concepts,
346,662 forms across 2,811 languages). For each concept, we checked
whether the language's forms share cognate sets with PMP (201 concepts)
or PAn (172 concepts) reconstructions.

**Results:**

| Variety | PMP Cognacy | Diff. vs Javanese | Type |
|---------|------------|-------------------|------|
| Old Javanese | 55.3% | +22.3% | historical baseline |
| Merina Malagasy | 40.8% | +7.8% | Type C (temporal) |
| Balinese | 40.3% | +7.3% | Type A (geographic) |
| Javanese (standard) | 33.0% | — | centre |
| Javanese (Yogyakarta) | 28.4% | -4.6% | sub-centre |
| Tengger (Ngadas) | 27.7% | -5.3% | Type A (small isolate) |
| Javanese (Malang) | 26.0% | -7.0% | sub-centre |

The gradient from Old Javanese (55.3%) to modern standard Javanese
(33.0%) quantifies 22.3% PMP cognacy erosion across ~1000 years of
Indianization and Islamization. Malagasy (40.8%) sits between Old
Javanese and modern Javanese, consistent with a ~1200 CE "snapshot"
that captured the language mid-way through this erosion process.

Balinese shows statistically borderline greater PMP retention than
standard Javanese (McNemar chi²=3.44, p=0.064), with 36 concepts
showing peripheral advantage (Balinese retains PMP cognate while
Javanese does not) vs 21 showing central advantage (Javanese retains,
Balinese does not).

### 3.2 Semantic Domain Concentration

The peripheral advantage is not uniform across vocabulary domains.
PMP cognacy rates by semantic domain:

| Domain | Balinese | Javanese | Difference |
|--------|----------|----------|------------|
| NATURE | 72.7% | 45.5% | **+27.2%** |
| KINSHIP | 25.0% | 12.5% | +12.5% |
| OTHER | 40.2% | 32.0% | +8.2% |
| BODY | 30.0% | 30.0% | 0.0% |
| ACTION | 20.8% | 29.2% | -8.4% |

Nature-domain terms (earth, stone, water, fire, tree, leaf, etc.)
show the largest peripheral advantage (+27.2%). This is consistent
with the hypothesis that *landscape vocabulary* — terms describing
the physical environment — is most resistant to prestige-driven
replacement, precisely because the landscape itself does not change
with cultural overlay.

### 3.3 The Indianization Wave (E033)

The cognacy gradient represents the endpoint of a process that E033
(Amien 2026c) reveals was non-monotonic. Analysis of Sanskrit/Indic
vocabulary ratios across 268 dated DHARMA inscriptions shows:

- Peak Indianization: Century 9 (Medang period), Indic ratio = 0.807
- Trough: Century 13 (Singhasari/early Majapahit), Indic ratio = 0.569
- Trend: Spearman rho = -0.211, p = 0.030 (significant decline)
- Pre-Indic term diversity: 1 term (C8-C9) → 5+ terms (C10-C11)

Indianization was a **wave** that crested in the 9th century and
receded, with pre-Indic vocabulary (*hyang*, *maṅhuri*, *gunung*,
*panumbas*) diversifying as the wave retreated. This reframes the
conventional periodization: the "end" of Hindu-Buddhism in Java (15th
century, Majapahit) was not a rupture but a formalization of substrate
reassertion that had been underway for centuries.

### 3.4 Phonological Substrate Fingerprint (E036)

Independent confirmation comes from the Hanacaraka script analysis
(E036, Amien & Gunawan 2026). The reduction from 33 Sanskrit consonants
to 20 Hanacaraka characters reveals a pre-existing phonological
inventory that aligns with Proto-Austronesian (~17 consonants), not
with Sanskrit (33). Lost categories: aspiration (8), retroflexes (5),
sibilant distinctions (2). The script was not passively adopted but
*actively fitted to a phonological system already in place* — the shape
of the glove reveals the shape of the hand.

## 4. Mortuary and Botanical Conservation

### 4.1 The Trunyan Case (Mortuary Structure)

Bali Aga of Trunyan practice *mepasah*: exposed above-ground burial,
body placed under the Taru Menyan tree (*Calophyllum inophyllum* or
local endemic), decomposition by natural processes, bones later
collected at site periphery. Key structural elements include:

1. No inhumation (not buried)
2. No cremation (not burned)
3. Botanical neutralization of decomposition odor
4. Tripartite cemetery classification by death-type
5. Volcanic context: site adjacent to active Mt. Batur
6. Belief: improper burial → volcanic eruption

Element 6 is remarkable: the community explicitly links mortuary
practice to volcanic activity. Whether causation runs from volcano
to ritual or ritual to volcano is undeterminable from ethnography
alone, but the association is robust.

**Cross-regional parallels:**

| Element | Trunyan | Toraja | Malagasy |
|---------|---------|--------|----------|
| Exposed/above-ground phase | ✓ (mepasah) | ✓ (tau-tau period) | ✓ (famadihana) |
| Secondary bone treatment | ✓ | ✓ (ma'nene) | ✓ (bone turning) |
| Aromatic botanical element | ✓ (Taru Menyan) | ? | ✓ (Canarium resin) |
| Community participation | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (mandatory) |
| Textile wrapping of bones | partial | ✓ | ✓ (silk shrouds) |

Malagasy *famadihana* (bone-turning, ~7-year cycle) and Toraja
*ma'nene* (annual bone-cleaning) are structurally homologous —
both involve secondary treatment of remains with strong community
participation and textile wrapping. This structural homology across
communities separated by 6,000 km of ocean points to a shared
Austronesian ancestral mortuary pattern.

### 4.2 The Botanical Substitution Chain — Revised (E044)

A critical finding of this study concerns the aromatic tree
ubiquitous at Javanese Muslim cemeteries: *Plumeria* spp. (kamboja/
frangipani). Informants consistently explain its presence as
odor-neutralization — functionally identical to the Trunyan
explanation for Taru Menyan.

However, **Plumeria is a New World species**, native to Mexico,
Central America, and the Caribbean. It was introduced to the
Philippines by Spanish colonists in the **1560s** and subsequently
spread to Indonesia (Plumeria Database; Wikipedia). It therefore
*cannot* be a pre-Hindu aromatic burial tree.

This requires a revision of the botanical substitution model from
three layers to four:

**Layer 1 — Pre-Hindu (~pre-400 CE):** Exposed burial with native
aromatic tree. Taru Menyan (Trunyan) survives as the last example.
The broader Austronesian candidate is ***Canarium* spp.** — a
resin-producing tree of the Burseraceae family used as ceremonial
incense across the Austronesian range:

| Species | Region | Ceremonial Use |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| *C. madagascariense* (ramy/haramy) | Madagascar | Ceremonial incense |
| *C. strictum* (dammar/sambrani) | Indonesia | Religious ceremonies |
| *C. commune* (kenari) | Java | Native aromatic nut tree |
| *C. indicum* | Melanesia | "Oldest tree crop," ceremony, canoe caulk |

Canarium is documented as "among the oldest and most important tree
crops in Melanesia" and an integral part of the earliest known
permanent agroforestry systems (World Agroforestry). Its presence in
both Indonesian and Malagasy ceremonial contexts supports an
Austronesian-era botanical connection.

**Layer 2 — Indianization (~400-1200 CE):** Inhumation becomes
dominant. Aromatic botanical element retained but function shifts from
"decomposition neutralizer" to "grave marker/purifier."

**Layer 3 — Islamization (~1200-1560 CE):** Inhumation reinforced.
Native aromatic tree tradition continues — but which species served
this role before Plumeria arrived remains unattested in Java proper.

**Layer 4 — Portuguese contact (~1560s onward):** Plumeria introduced
from the Americas. Adopted at cemeteries because of similar aromatic
properties and fast growth. Replaces whatever native species
(Canarium? endemic aromatic?) had served the burial function.

**The diagnostic prediction** is confirmed: Plumeria does NOT appear
in Malagasy burial contexts (migration predates introduction by >300
years), but *Canarium* IS present as ceremonial aromatic in Madagascar.
The absence of Plumeria and presence of Canarium in Madagascar is
exactly what the 4-layer model predicts.

### 4.3 Sacred Forests and Botanical Taboos (E044)

Additional structural homology appears in the treatment of vegetation
around burial sites:

- **Madagascar:** Sacred forests (*ala masina*) around tombs are
  strictly protected; it is forbidden to cut trees or burn dead wood.
  35 woody species are unique to fady-protected tomb forests.
  Ancestors are believed to reside in sacred trees (*hazofaly*).
- **Java/Bali:** Sacred groves around temples and cemeteries;
  botanical taboos protect specific species; ancestors associated
  with waringin (banyan) trees.

The parallel is structural (protected forest around burial/sacred
site) and functional (botanical taboo preserves species), supporting
a shared Austronesian ancestral practice.

### 4.4 Mortuary Plants vs Epigraphic Record (E035)

E035 screened 268 DHARMA inscriptions for 15 plant types. 249/268
(92.9%) contain botanical terms. However, the mortuary-specific
plants — *menyan* (benzoin) and *kamboja* — are **completely absent**
from the epigraphic record. This despite menyan being the namesake
of the most archaic surviving burial site (Taru Menyan, Trunyan).

This absence is diagnostic: mortuary botanical knowledge existed
entirely in the **oral tradition register**, separate from the
epigraphic register that recorded royal land grants and ritual
prescriptions. The prasasti record the Indianized surface; the
mortuary practice lived underneath, transmitted orally, and therefore
invisible to epigraphy-based historiography.

## 5. The Organic Civilization and Genre Taphonomy

### 5.1 The Bamboo Civilization (E040)

E040 systematically searched 268 DHARMA inscriptions for references
to organic and lithic building materials. The results are striking:

- **Organic materials:** 170/268 (63.4%) inscriptions mention organic
  materials (daun 48%, atap 32%, kayu 26%, ijuk 18%, bambu 10%)
- **Lithic materials:** 73/268 (27.2%)
- **Organic-only:** 103 inscriptions
- **Lithic-only:** 6 inscriptions
- **Binomial test:** p < 0.0001

The prasasti themselves confirm that Old Javanese civilization was
overwhelmingly organic — wood, bamboo, palm fiber, thatch. The
archaeological "blank" before the stone temple period (post-8th
century) reflects not the absence of civilization but the absence
of non-perishable materials. A bamboo civilization on volcanic soil
leaves effectively zero archaeological trace.

### 5.2 Genre Taphonomy

E040 also revealed an unexpected pattern: century-8 inscriptions
mention organic materials at only 13%, while century-9-11 inscriptions
mention them at 68-91%. This is not because the civilization changed —
it is because the **genre of inscription** changed. C8 inscriptions
are predominantly short Sanskrit-language labels (sima format:
boundary markers), while C9+ inscriptions are detailed Old Javanese
descriptions of land grants, occupations, and economic activity.

This constitutes a **genre taphonomy**: the form of the textual
record acts as a filter on what cultural information is transmitted
to us. Sanskrit-genre inscriptions are blind to organic material
culture; Old Javanese-genre inscriptions reveal it. The material
reality did not change — the recording technology did.

### 5.3 The P1↔P8 Bridge

The organic civilization finding (E040) bridges two otherwise separate
arguments:

- **P1** (Amien 2026a): Volcanic burial physically destroys organic
  archaeological evidence at 2.4-6.2 mm/year
- **P8** (Amien & Gunawan 2026): Linguistic substrate detection
  reveals non-Sanskrit vocabulary for everyday activities (action
  verbs dominate top-50 ML-detected substrates at 46%)

These are describing the **same civilization** from two angles:
the organic-material world that is physically buried (P1) is the
same world described in pre-Sanskrit vocabulary (P8) and recorded in
Old Javanese sima inscriptions (E040). Three independent channels —
taphonomic, linguistic, and epigraphic — converge on one organic
civilization rendered invisible by multiple mechanisms.

## 6. East Java as Natural Laboratory

[Condensed from v0.1 §5]

East Java presents a natural laboratory for PCF because it contains
four mutually unintelligible Austronesian languages (Javanese,
Madurese, Balinese, Osing) plus one archaic volcanic isolate (Tengger)
in a single geographic area. Key observations:

**Geological substrate drives linguistic maintenance:** Madura
(limestone) vs Java (volcanic) differ geologically, producing
materially different lifeways, reinforcing linguistic distinctness
across millennia of contact. This is the VCS argument in reverse:
non-volcanic substrate → different cultural toolkit → maintained
separation.

**Osing as Type B peripheral:** Remnant of Blambangan, the last Hindu
kingdom on Java (fell ~1770s). Extreme eastern periphery + Hindu
identity maintenance + contact with Bali = triple isolation mechanism.
Prediction: Osing preserves pre-Islamic Javanese better than any
standard Javanese dialect. (Testable with ABVD cognacy analysis.)

**Tengger as scale paradox exemplar:** Hindu + volcanic isolate
= apparent "double fossil," yet E043 shows *lower* PMP cognacy than
standard Javanese. This confirms §2.3: small isolates drift, they
don't conserve. The "triple fossil" prediction from v0.1 was wrong
for lexical conservation (though Tengger ritual vocabulary may still
be archaic — untested).

**Multiple substrates implication:** East Java evidence suggests there
was no single "pre-Austronesian substrate" for Java. Different
geological zones hosted different pre-existing populations. This
constrains P8-type substrate recovery: do not assume one Sulawesi
substrate either.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 PCF as Method

The Peripheral Conservatism Framework is not a claim about specific
cultural content — it is a **methodological proposition** about where
to look for pre-Hindu substrates. Its core logic:

1. Centres overwrite themselves through prestige-driven replacement
2. Peripheries escape overwriting (partially) through isolation
3. Temporal peripheries (Madagascar) provide dateable calibration
4. Computational methods (cognacy analysis, corpus screening, ML
   substrate detection) scale the recovery across large datasets

### 7.2 Implications

The implication extends beyond archaeology: the living communities
of Trunyan, Toraja, and Banyuwangi are not cultural relics. They are
**active archives** of a Nusantaran deep past that volcanic burial
has erased from the material record. The methodological inversion —
looking for the most conservative practice at the periphery rather
than the oldest evidence at the centre — is particularly powerful
for Nusantara because:

1. Volcanic taphonomy ensures centres have the *least* physical
   evidence (P1)
2. Cultural overwriting ensures centres have the *least* intangible
   substrate (this paper)
3. Genre taphonomy ensures the textual record is biased toward
   Indianized surface (E040)

All three biases are independently demonstrated and jointly explain
the apparent archaeological "blank" of pre-Hindu western Indonesia.

### 7.3 Limitations

- E043 cognacy analysis uses basic vocabulary (210 concepts) only;
  formal register (Krama/Alus) vocabulary is not captured in ABVD
- Malagasy departure date (~1200 CE) provides only a terminus ante
  quem; elements may be much older
- Trunyan mortuary practice may be locally innovated rather than
  ancestrally retained — the exposed burial pattern could reflect
  Bali Aga-specific adaptation to Batur caldera conditions
- The Canarium connection, while suggestive, is circumstantial;
  systematic ethnobotanical fieldwork is needed
- The scale paradox (§2.3) needs testing across more peripheries

## 8. Conclusion

We have proposed the Peripheral Conservatism Framework as a
systematic methodology for recovering pre-Hindu Nusantaran cultural
substrates from living peripheral communities. Three independent
evidence channels — lexical conservation (E043), mortuary ritual
structure, and botanical burial practice (E044) — converge on a
consistent pattern: peripheral communities retain archaic
Austronesian elements that centres have lost through successive
overwriting.

The framework yields five principal findings:

1. Balinese retains 7.3% more PMP cognacy than standard Javanese,
   concentrated in nature-domain vocabulary (+27.2%)
2. Malagasy (40.8% PMP cognacy) provides a dateable ~1200 CE
   calibration point between Old Javanese (55.3%) and modern
   Javanese (33.0%)
3. Plumeria (kamboja), the iconic Javanese cemetery tree, is a
   post-1560 New World introduction; the genuine pan-Austronesian
   aromatic is Canarium spp.
4. The Indianization of Java was a wave (peak C9, rho=-0.211,
   p=0.030), not a permanent transformation
5. Pre-Hindu Java was an organic civilization (63.4% organic
   mentions in prasasti) whose material culture was destroyed by
   the same volcanic processes that buried it

These findings support a program of systematic substrate recovery
from Nusantaran peripheries using computational methods, with
Madagascar as the indispensable calibration point.
