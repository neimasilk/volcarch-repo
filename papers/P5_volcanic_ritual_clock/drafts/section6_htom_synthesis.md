The preceding sections have traced the slametan death sequence from its uniquely Javanese numerical identity (Section 2), through its pre-Indic cosmological substrate (Section 3), its pan-Austronesian structural inheritance (Section 4), to its systematic correspondence with forensic decomposition stages in volcanic soil (Section 5). In this section, we draw these threads together into a unified model that situates mortuary ritual within the broader taphonomic regime of volcanic landscapes.

## 6.1 The Ritual Timescale: 1000 Days

The slametan cycle operates on what we term the ritual timescale. Over approximately 1000 days (2.7 years), the body of the deceased undergoes progressive dissolution in the acidic volcanic soil of the Javanese lowlands. The slametan marks this progression at empirically significant thresholds: the end of the fresh stage (3 days), the peak of putrefactive transformation (7 days), the near-complete loss of soft tissue (40 days), advanced skeletonisation (100 days), and the dissolution of bone mineral itself (1000 days). At the endpoint — *nyewu* — the body has, in Geertz's (1960: 72) formulation, 'completely decayed to dust,' and the tradition holds that the soul 'will no longer return to the family' (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 440).

The ritual timescale thus constitutes a culturally encoded taphonomic calendar: a sequence of social performances calibrated to the physical transformation of the dead. The concept of *kasampurnan* (perfection, completion) that organises the slametan sequence is, in this reading, a theory of decomposition — each ceremony 'perfects' the component of the body that the soil is currently consuming.

The Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna, a traditional Javanese reference work, codifies this calendar in computational form. Entry No. 332 presents a calculation table that maps the day and *pasaran* (five-day market week) of death to the precise calendrical dates for each subsequent ceremony through *nyewu* (Soemodidjojo 1994: 245--246). The entry defines *nyewu* explicitly as 'selamatan hari keseribu terhitung dari meninggal dunianya si mati' — the ceremony on the thousandth day counted from the death of the deceased. That this sequence is treated as a fixed computation — an invariant function of the date of death — implies that the intervals were understood as natural and necessary, not arbitrary or negotiable.

Immediately preceding this calendrical table in the Primbon is Entry No. 331, which provides a prayer for cases in which 'the corpse smells bad' (*jika jenasah berbau busuk*). The juxtaposition of a decomposition-management entry with the selamatan calculation table is unlikely to be coincidental. It reflects an indigenous framework in which ritual timing and bodily decay are conceptually contiguous: the Primbon moves from the problem of decomposition to the solution of the ritual calendar without transition, as elements of a single system.

## 6.2 The Geological Timescale: 1000 Years

A second, slower taphonomic process operates on the same landscape. The volcanic arc of Java produces tephra — ash falls, lahars, and pyroclastic deposits — at rates that, in the alluvial plains surrounding major stratovolcanoes, translate to average sedimentation of approximately 3.6--3.7 mm per year (Amien 2026a, 2026b). At this rate, a surface site is buried beneath one metre of deposit within approximately 270 years, and beneath 3.6 metres — well beyond the depth of agricultural disturbance or casual discovery — within approximately 1000 years.

This geological sedimentation creates a systematic bias in the archaeological record. Open-air sites in volcanic Java older than a few centuries are invisible to surface survey; their discovery depends on accidental exposure through erosion, construction, or deep excavation. The temples of Sambisari (buried under 6.5 metres of lahar deposits from Merapi), Kedulan (discovered during well-digging at 4 metres depth), and the Dwarapala statues of Singhasari (partially buried under volcanic sediment) illustrate the mechanism. These are monumental stone structures; domestic wooden architecture and organic materials fare far worse.

## 6.3 The Convergence: One Volcanic Driver

The critical insight of the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis is that these two timescales — ritual (years) and geological (centuries to millennia) — are driven by a single volcanic process. The tephra that weathers into acidic Andosol is the same material that accumulates as the sedimentary matrix burying archaeological sites. Volcanism simultaneously creates the soil chemistry that dissolves bodies rapidly, generating the empirical basis for the slametan calendar, and produces the depositional environment that progressively removes archaeological sites from visibility.

This convergence generates what we term the Hypothetical Taphonomic Overlay Model (H-TOM): a framework in which ritual and geological taphonomy are understood as complementary expressions of the same environmental forcing.

**Table 5: Two Timescales of Volcanic Taphonomic Bias**

| Parameter | Ritual Timescale | Geological Timescale |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Duration** | ~1,000 days (~2.7 years) | ~1,000 years |
| **Process** | Body decomposition in acidic soil | Site burial by volcanic sedimentation |
| **Driver** | Tephra-derived Andosol (pH 4.5--5.5) | Tephra deposition (~3.6 mm/yr) |
| **Endpoint** | Body dissolved to dust (*nyewu*) | Site buried >3.5 m (invisible to survey) |
| **Cultural response** | Slametan ritual calendar | None (process invisible to single generation) |
| **Evidence type destroyed** | Organic remains, bone, grave goods | Settlement patterns, spatial relationships |

The symmetry between the two timescales is suggestive but not claimed as causal. We do not argue that the number 1000 was chosen because of sedimentation rates — the geological process operates over centuries, far beyond the observational horizon of any individual or community. Rather, the parallel serves to highlight the scope of volcanic taphonomic bias: the same environment that calibrates the ritual clock also destroys the material evidence of the people who kept it. The living culture records, in ritual, the very process that erases its own material past.

## 6.4 Implications for Archaeological Interpretation

The H-TOM framework carries several implications for how we interpret the archaeological record of volcanic Java.

First, it suggests that the slametan cycle constitutes a form of 'living taphonomic evidence' — a cultural practice that preserves, in ritual form, empirical knowledge about decomposition that the archaeological record itself cannot contain because the very process described by the ritual destroys the evidence. Just as Aboriginal Australian fire management encodes ecological knowledge accumulated over millennia (Gammage 2011), the slametan may encode taphonomic knowledge accumulated through generations of grave visitation in volcanic soil.

Second, it reinforces the argument that archaeological absence in volcanic Java should not be interpreted as evidence of low population density or cultural marginality. The archaeological invisibility of Javanese sites is a predictable consequence of a specific geological environment, and the persistence of elaborate mortuary ritual is itself evidence that the 'missing' communities existed and sustained complex cultural systems.

Third, it opens a new interdisciplinary research direction that we might term 'taphonomic ethnography': the systematic study of living cultural practices for the environmental information they may encode. If mortuary timing in Java reflects decomposition rates, analogous encodings may exist in other ritual systems calibrated to other environmental processes.
