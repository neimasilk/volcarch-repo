## 7.1 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the strength of the claims advanced in this paper.

First, the decomposition timelines cited in Section 5 are drawn from the general forensic taphonomy literature, conducted in temperate and subtropical environments. No published decomposition study has been conducted specifically in Javanese volcanic Andosol. While the environmental parameters of lowland Java — temperature, humidity, soil acidity — fall within the range of conditions studied in the forensic literature, and while the accumulated degree-day (ADD) framework provides a principled basis for extrapolation, the absence of site-specific data means that our interval-to-stage mappings remain predictions rather than empirical confirmations. This represents the most significant gap in the evidentiary chain.

Second, the slametan intervals as documented today may not be identical to their historical antecedents. Oral traditions are subject to drift, standardisation, and rationalisation over centuries. The possibility that the current intervals are a relatively recent codification — rather than a faithful transmission of ancient practice — cannot be excluded on the basis of available evidence. However, three observations mitigate this concern: (a) multiple Indonesian scholars independently classify the intervals as pre-dating both Islamic and Hindu-Buddhist influence (Aizid 2015; Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020; Sholihah et al. 2023); (b) the intervals are consistent across geographically separated Javanese communities, suggesting a deep and stable tradition rather than a recent innovation; and (c) the Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna treats the intervals as a fixed computational system, implying that they were regarded as invariant by the time of codification.

Third, the Pulotu database codes beliefs and practices at the culture level, not the community or village level. Variation within cultures — between coastal and highland communities, between urban and rural populations, between communities with different burial practices — is not captured. The 'full mortuary package' as defined in this paper is therefore a coarse-grained measure; finer-grained analysis might reveal significant internal variation that our cross-cultural approach obscures.

Fourth, the ISRIC SoilGrids data used for the pH cross-reference represent modern soil conditions. Historical soil pH may have differed, particularly in areas subject to intensive agriculture, irrigation, or land-use change. However, the primary determinant of Andosol acidity — the mineralogy of the volcanic parent material — is geologically stable on millennial timescales, and the range of pH values characteristic of Javanese volcanic soils (4.5--5.5) is well-established in the pedological literature.

## 7.2 Falsification Criteria

The Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis generates several testable predictions whose failure would weaken or falsify the argument.

**Prediction 1 (Strong test):** Controlled decomposition experiments in Javanese Andosol should produce a stage sequence whose timing is broadly consistent with the slametan intervals. Specifically, soft tissue should be substantially consumed by approximately 40 days, skeletonisation should be advanced by approximately 100 days, and bone mineral dissolution should be substantial by approximately 1000 days, in shallow-buried remains at ambient tropical temperatures. If these conditions produce a decomposition trajectory radically different from the slametan schedule — for instance, if bone is fully preserved for decades — the hypothesis is falsified.

**Prediction 2 (Cross-cultural test):** Cultures practising shallow burial in environments with substantially different soil chemistry should exhibit different mortuary interval timing. Specifically, communities in limestone or coral-derived alkaline soils (pH 7--8), where hydroxyapatite is chemically stable for centuries to millennia, should either lack a fixed terminal ceremony analogous to *nyewu* or should set this ceremony at a substantially later date. If alkaline-soil cultures exhibit the same 1000-day mortuary endpoint as volcanic-soil cultures, the environmental calibration hypothesis is undermined.

**Prediction 3 (Primbon test):** If close reading of the full Primbon corpus, or of other Javanese esoteric texts, reveals an explicit theological or cosmological rationale for the specific numerical intervals — one that is independent of any reference to bodily state — the hypothesis would need to be modified to accommodate both taphonomic and doctrinal inputs. The evidence presented in this paper shows that the Primbon's language about the body is observational rather than theological (Section 5), but the possibility of additional doctrinal layers cannot be excluded without comprehensive textual analysis.

**Prediction 4 (Grave visitation test):** If ethnographic fieldwork demonstrates that Javanese communities historically did not practise periodic grave visitation during the slametan cycle — i.e., that the ceremonies were conducted exclusively in domestic settings with no contact with the burial site — the mechanism by which empirical observation of decomposition could have been accumulated would be weakened. The Primbon's inclusion of a grave visitation protocol (Entry No. 334) suggests that such visitation was institutionalised, but the geographic and temporal generality of this practice requires further documentation.

## 7.3 The 'Just-So Story' Objection

An inevitable criticism of any hypothesis linking ritual practice to environmental process is that it risks being a 'just-so story' — a retrospective narrative that explains observed patterns without generating testable predictions. We take this objection seriously and have attempted to address it in three ways.

First, the hypothesis was generated from the data, not imposed upon it. The correspondence between slametan intervals and decomposition stages was not assumed *a priori* but emerged from the independent comparison of two bodies of evidence: forensic taphonomy literature and Javanese ethnographic sources. The fact that the Javanese tradition's own language about the body at each interval matches the forensic prediction — without any indication that the ethnographic sources were informed by forensic science — constitutes evidence that requires explanation.

Second, the hypothesis generates specific, falsifiable predictions (Section 7.2) that can be tested with field data. A just-so story, by definition, is not falsifiable; the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis is.

Third, we do not claim exact correspondence. The slametan intervals are not 'correct' forensic predictions; they are approximate cultural encodings that reflect the ordering and approximate magnitude of decomposition stages as observable to non-specialist observers over generations. The claim is modest: that the sequence is consistent with empirical observation, not that it constitutes a scientific measurement.

## 7.4 Broader Significance

If the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis is sustained by future empirical testing, it carries implications beyond the Javanese case. It suggests that mortuary ritual timing, in societies that practise earth burial and periodic grave visitation, may constitute a previously unrecognised source of environmental information — a form of 'taphonomic ethnography' in which living cultural practices encode empirical knowledge about local decomposition conditions. This possibility merits systematic investigation across the Austronesian world and, potentially, beyond it.

The hypothesis also contributes to ongoing debates about the relationship between 'belief' and 'observation' in non-Western knowledge systems. The slametan tradition is typically classified as religious ritual; its practitioners would not describe it as environmental science. Yet the tradition's own language — its inventory of tissue types, its claims about bodily states at specific intervals, its computational calendar — is observational in character. The distinction between 'ritual knowledge' and 'empirical knowledge' may, in cases like this, be less clear than conventional disciplinary boundaries suggest.
