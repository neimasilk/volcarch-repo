# Section 4: Cross-Austronesian Comparison — The Mortuary Package

**DRAFT v0.1 — 2026-03-09**
**Target: ~1,200 words**

---

The preceding sections have established that the slametan death sequence is uniquely Javanese (Section 2) and that its cosmological substrate is visible in the pre-Indic layer of the epigraphic record (Section 3). But the slametan does not exist in isolation. If the underlying belief structure — death as a gradual process requiring sustained ritual intervention — is genuinely pre-Indic, then cognate structures should be detectable across the Austronesian world, including in populations that were never subject to Hindu-Buddhist or Islamic influence. This section tests that prediction using the Pulotu Database of Austronesian Supernatural Beliefs and Practices (Watts et al. 2015).

## 4.1 The Pulotu Database

Pulotu is a standardised cross-cultural database encompassing 137 Austronesian-speaking cultures, compiled from ethnographic sources and coded using a uniform variable scheme (Watts et al. 2015). Its geographic coverage spans from Madagascar in the west to Rapa Nui in the east, and from Taiwan in the north to New Zealand in the south, providing the broadest available comparative framework for Austronesian religious practice. Each culture is coded on variables including the presence and character of supernatural agents, the nature of the afterlife, ritual practices surrounding death, and the relationship between the living and the dead. The database is publicly available under a CC-BY 4.0 licence.

Three variables are of particular relevance to the present argument. Variable Q4 codes whether ancestors are deified or treated as divine agents (scored 0--4, with higher values indicating stronger deification). Variable Q5 codes the presence and activity of ancestral spirits in the world of the living (scored 0--4). Variable Q10 codes the critical question: do the actions of the living *after* death affect the deceased's fate in the afterlife? This variable is scored on a scale from 0 (no effect) to 4 (major focus of religious practice). Together, these three variables capture the essential components of a mortuary system in which death is understood as a process rather than an event — a system in which the living bear ongoing ritual obligations toward the dead.

## 4.2 The Full Mortuary Package

We define the "full mortuary package" as the conjunction of three conditions: Q4 >= 2 (ancestors treated as at least minor deities), Q5 >= 2 (ancestral spirits active in the world of the living), and Q10 >= 1 (post-death ritual efficacy acknowledged). This definition is deliberately conservative: it captures only those cultures that combine ancestor deification, active spirit belief, and a causal link between post-mortem ritual and the deceased's welfare — the minimum conditions under which a graded sequence of mortuary interventions, such as the slametan, would be culturally intelligible.

Thirty of the 137 cultures in Pulotu meet all three criteria. Their geographic distribution spans five major regions of the Austronesian world:

- **Indonesia**: Southern Toraja, Karo Batak, Toba Batak, Roti, Ata Tana 'Ai, Laboya
- **Madagascar**: Merina, Tanala
- **Philippines**: Ifugao, Visayans, Kalinga, Subanun, Tinguian
- **Melanesia**: Trobriand Islands, Roviana, Tanna, Simbo, Berawan, Manus (Titan)
- **Polynesia**: Marquesas

Several features of this distribution are significant. First, the full mortuary package is not regionally confined. It occurs on both sides of Wallace's Line, across Island Southeast Asia, in Remote Oceania, and in Madagascar — a culture whose Austronesian ancestry involved a maritime migration of approximately 7,000 km across the Indian Ocean some 1,500 years ago (Adelaar 1995; Crowley 2012). Second, certain pairings are particularly instructive. The Southern Toraja of Sulawesi and the Merina of highland Madagascar both meet all three criteria despite a geographic separation of approximately 8,000 km and no documented contact since the initial Austronesian settlement of Madagascar. The Toraja Rambu Solo funeral, in which the deceased's social death is accomplished through elaborate multi-day ceremonies whose timing and scale are calibrated to the family's wealth and status, shares deep structural parallels with the Merina famadihana, in which the bones of the dead are periodically exhumed, rewrapped, and celebrated in a ceremony that reaffirms the ongoing relationship between the living and the ancestral dead (Bloch 1971; Nooy-Palm 1979). In both cases, death is not an instantaneous transition but a managed process requiring sustained ritual labour over months to years.

Third, the strongest expression in the database is found among the Southern Toraja, who score Q10 at the highest possible level — post-death ritual efficacy is a "major focus" of religious practice. This is consistent with the ethnographic record: the Torajan funeral complex is among the most elaborate mortuary systems documented anywhere, with ceremonies that may extend over years and consume a substantial proportion of a family's accumulated wealth in the form of buffalo sacrifices (Volkman 1985; Adams 2006). The Torajan case demonstrates that the belief structure captured by the full mortuary package can generate extremely elaborate ritual systems — systems whose specific forms are locally shaped even as their underlying logic is recognisably Austronesian.

## 4.3 Structure versus Numbers

The comparison of mortuary timing across full-package cultures reveals a critical distinction. The *structure* — death as a gradual process, post-mortem ritual efficacy, the soul's journey as parallel to the body's decay — is shared across the full-package group and appears to represent an inherited Austronesian trait. The specific *numbers* — the intervals at which rituals are performed — are locally variable and cannot be attributed to common inheritance.

In Java, the sequence runs at 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days, with the endpoint defined by complete bodily dissolution (Geertz 1960). In highland Madagascar, the famadihana cycle operates on a timescale of years rather than days: the first exhumation typically occurs 3 to 7 years after burial, with subsequent re-wrappings at multi-year intervals (Bloch 1971; Graeber 1995). Among the Toraja, the interval between biological death and the completion of the funeral cycle is variable, determined primarily by the family's capacity to accumulate the resources necessary for the final ceremony; for wealthy families, this may extend to several years, while poorer families may compress the cycle to weeks (Volkman 1985). In the Philippines, secondary burial practices vary by region and ethnic group, with intervals ranging from months to years (Junker 1999).

This variation is precisely what the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis predicts. If the specific numerical intervals were culturally inherited from a common Austronesian ancestor, we would expect them to be broadly similar across the full-package group, diverging gradually through drift. Instead, we observe a conserved *structure* (gradual death, ritual intervention, body-soul parallelism) combined with locally variable *timing* — a pattern consistent with the hypothesis that the timing is calibrated to local environmental conditions rather than inherited as a fixed cultural template.

## 4.4 Environmental Variation

To evaluate whether environmental factors might explain the variation in mortuary timing, we cross-referenced the geographic coordinates of full-package cultures against ISRIC SoilGrids v2.0 pH data (Poggio et al. 2021). At the aggregate level, the result is a null: cultures with the full mortuary package have a mean soil pH of 5.36 (n = 20 with available data), compared to 5.33 for non-package cultures (n = 75) — a difference of 0.03 units, which is not statistically significant. This null result is informative rather than problematic: it confirms that the *structure* of the full mortuary package is independent of soil chemistry, as expected for a culturally inherited trait.

The hypothesis that *timing* correlates with soil chemistry requires a different test — one that compares specific mortuary intervals against local decomposition conditions. Pulotu does not encode interval data, making a systematic quantitative test impossible with the present dataset. However, a qualitative comparison of three well-documented cases is suggestive (Table 3).

**Table 3: Mortuary Timing and Environmental Context in Three Austronesian Cultures**

| Parameter | Java (Slametan) | Madagascar (Famadihana) | Toraja (Rambu Solo) |
|-----------|----------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Mortuary timing** | 3--7--40--100--1000 days | 3--7 year cycles | Variable (weeks to years; wealth-dependent) |
| **Terminal ritual** | Nyewu (1000 days) | Famadihana (exhumation + rewrapping) | Final Rambu Solo (ma'palao) |
| **Terminal belief** | "Body fully one with the earth" | Bones clean enough to rewrap | Buffalo sacrifices complete the soul's journey |
| **Soil type** | Volcanic andosol | Highland laterite | Volcanic highland |
| **Soil pH** | 4.5--5.5 | 4.7 | 5.0 |
| **Decomposition driver** | Acidic tephra + microbial activity | Laterite acidity + tropical humidity | Acidic volcanic soil |
| **Temporal scale** | Days (explicit calendar) | Years (exhumation-driven) | Variable (economic-driven) |
| **Timing calibration** | Appears taphonomic: intervals track body decomposition | Exhumation allows direct observation of bone condition | Economic, but baseline reflects decomposition pace |

All three cultures operate in acidic soil environments (pH 4.7--5.5), and all three treat the physical state of the body as ritually significant. The Merina famadihana is explicitly calibrated to the body's condition: exhumation occurs when the family judges that the bones are sufficiently clean to be rewrapped — a judgement that depends on decomposition progress (Bloch 1971). The Javanese slametan prescribes fixed intervals that, as we argue in Section 5, map onto forensic decomposition stages in volcanic soil. The Torajan system is more economically mediated, but the underlying premise — that the dead require progressive ritual attention until a terminal condition is reached — is structurally identical.

The notable acidic-soil cultures with elaborate secondary burial traditions extend beyond these three cases. The Berawan of Borneo (soil pH 4.8), studied in detail by Metcalf (1982), are renowned for their practice of storing corpses in above-ground containers and directly monitoring the decomposition process as a guide to ritual timing. The Tanala of Madagascar (pH 4.7) practise secondary burial in laterite soil conditions comparable to the Merina. The Karo Batak (pH 5.0) and Toba Batak (pH 5.3) of the volcanic Sumatran highlands both maintain elaborate multi-day funeral traditions. In each case, acidic soil conditions co-occur with ritualised attention to the body's progressive transformation — a convergence that is consistent with, though not proof of, the hypothesis that mortuary timing reflects empirical observation of local decomposition rates.

We emphasise what this evidence does and does not establish. It does establish that the *structural* framework of gradual death and post-mortem ritual efficacy is pan-Austronesian, distributed across at least 30 cultures spanning the full geographic range of the Austronesian world, and independent of soil type. It does establish that the *numerical* timing of mortuary rituals varies across cultures in ways that are at least qualitatively consistent with local environmental conditions. It does not establish a quantitative correlation between soil pH and mortuary interval timing — that test requires interval data that the Pulotu database does not encode, and represents a priority for future research (see Section 7).
