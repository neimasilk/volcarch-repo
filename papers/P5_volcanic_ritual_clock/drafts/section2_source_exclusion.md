The Javanese death slametan follows a fixed sequence of communal ritual meals held at 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days after death, with additional commemorations at the first and second anniversaries (Geertz 1960; Koentjaraningrat 1994). The sequence is known locally as *nelung dina* (3), *mitung dina* (7), *matang puluh* (40), *nyatus* (100), *mendhak sepisan* (1 year), *mendhak pindo* (2 years), and *nyewu* (1000) (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 433--435). Each ceremony escalates in scale: the *nyewu* typically involves the largest gathering, the most elaborate food offerings, and the completion of the Quranic recitation cycle begun at *geblag* (the day of burial) (Sholihah et al. 2023).

Because modern Javanese society is overwhelmingly Muslim, with significant Hindu-Buddhist historical influence, the immediate question is whether these intervals derive from any of the three major imported religious traditions. If they do, the intervals tell us about imported theology; if they do not, they constitute evidence of an indigenous Javanese system whose internal logic requires explanation. We examine each tradition in turn.

## 2.1 Hindu sources

The Hindu mortuary rite (*antyeshti*) and its associated post-death observances (*shraddha*) follow a 10--13 day cycle. The *sapindikarana* ceremony, which formally incorporates the deceased into the company of ancestors (*pitrs*), is performed on the 12th or 13th day after death (Knipe 1977; Parry 1994). Subsequent annual observances occur during *Pitru Paksha*, a 16-day period in the Hindu lunar calendar dedicated to ancestral offerings.

Crucially, the Hindu system contains no 40-day, 100-day, or 1000-day interval. The shraddha cycle operates on a base-10/12/13 arithmetic that bears no structural resemblance to the slametan sequence. The only numerical overlap is trivial: both traditions mark a third day, but the Hindu *tritiya* serves a different ritual function (lighting the funeral pyre or completing cremation rites) than the Javanese *nelung dina* (first communal prayer for the departed soul).

Indian Hindu communities in Java during the Mataram and Majapahit periods (8th--15th centuries CE) would have practiced shraddha according to their own calendrical system. The Old Javanese inscriptions in the DHARMA corpus confirm the use of the Saka calendar and Sanskrit ritual terminology in court contexts (Griffiths et al. 2020). However, none of the 268 inscriptions examined in this study contains a reference to the 3--7--40--100--1000 day sequence. The slametan intervals are absent from the epigraphic record of Hindu-Javanese civilization.

## 2.2 Buddhist sources

The Buddhist concept of the intermediate state (*antarabhava* or *bardo*) provides a 49-day framework for the soul's transition between death and rebirth. During this period, prayers and merit-making are performed every seven days for seven weeks (7 × 7 = 49 days) (Cuevas 2003). Some East Asian Buddhist traditions extend this with a 100-day observance, though scholars have argued that this reflects Confucian rather than Buddhist influence (Teiser 1988).

The Buddhist system thus overlaps with the slametan at two points: the 7-day unit and the 100-day mark. However, it diverges fundamentally in three ways. First, the Buddhist 7-day cycle repeats seven times (days 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42, 49), whereas the Javanese system uses 7 as a single marker followed by 40. Second, the Buddhist framework has no 40-day interval. Third, and most importantly, there is no 1000-day observance in any Buddhist scriptural tradition. The *nyewu* — which Geertz (1960: 72) identifies as the point at which "the body has completely decayed to dust" — has no Buddhist equivalent.

The partial overlap with the 7-day and 100-day marks suggests possible Buddhist influence on individual elements, but the complete sequence as a system cannot be derived from Buddhist doctrine.

## 2.3 Islamic sources

The Islamic mourning period (*hidad*) is limited to three days, with an exception for widows (four months and ten days, per Quran 2:234). The practices of communal prayer gatherings at 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days after death are well-established in Javanese Muslim communities, but they are explicitly classified as *bid'ah* (innovation) by Islamic jurisprudence (Woodward 1989: 155--157).

Indonesian Islamic scholars have consistently distinguished between these practices as *adat* (local custom) and *syariat* (religious law). The Al-Jami'ah journal has published detailed analyses concluding that "the slametan kematian is not mandated by Islamic scripture but represents a cultural practice that has been given Islamic content through the substitution of Quranic recitations for pre-existing ritual forms" (cf. Suwito et al. 2015). Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim (2020: 441) document this process explicitly: "In the old belief, the ceremony was performed by making offerings to supernatural forces (spirits, subtle beings, gods). Islam flexibly gave new color to those ceremonies with the designation *kenduren* or *selamatan*."

The key insight from the Islamic perspective is one of substitution, not invention: Islam replaced the verbal content of pre-existing ceremonies (mantras became Quranic recitations, offerings to spirits became *sedekah* for neighbours) while preserving the temporal structure and numerical intervals intact.

This interpretation is further supported by Aizid (2015: 149), who states directly: "The origin of the death slametan existed before Hinduism-Buddhism came to Indonesia. After the arrival of Hindu-Buddhist teachings, the death slametan tradition changed in its mantras and prayers, and after the arrival of Islam, the mantras underwent significant adjustment — replacing them with prayers according to Islamic teachings."

## 2.4 The verdict: a uniquely Javanese sequence

The complete 3--7--40--100--1000 day sequence cannot be derived from any single imported religious tradition:

- **Hinduism** provides none of the intervals beyond the trivial day-3 overlap.
- **Buddhism** provides 7 and possibly 100, but not 40 or 1000.
- **Islam** explicitly classifies the intervals as non-Islamic (*bid'ah*) and recognizes them as pre-existing local custom.

The most parsimonious explanation is that the numerical sequence is indigenous to Java, predating all three imported traditions. Multiple Indonesian scholars have reached this conclusion independently: the death slametan "was known by Javanese society when they followed animist and dynamist beliefs" (Sholihah et al. 2023: 362); it represents "traditional local culture of the Javanese people" that "can be classified as Javanese culture" (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 440).

If the intervals are neither Hindu, Buddhist, nor Islamic in origin, what generated these specific numbers? The sequence is not arbitrary: it is not based on any obvious mathematical progression (it is neither geometric nor arithmetic), and it does not correspond to any astronomical cycle. In the following sections, we argue that the intervals reflect empirical observation of a physical process — the decomposition of the human body in Javanese volcanic soil — and that the slametan death cycle is, in effect, a taphonomic calendar.
