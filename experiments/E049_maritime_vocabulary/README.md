# E049: Maritime & Nature Vocabulary Conservation Across Austronesian Peripheries

## Hypothesis
Maritime and nature vocabulary shows higher PMP cognacy in peripheral communities
(Balinese, Malagasy) than in the Javanese centre — indicating the "organic civilization"
was also a "maritime civilization."

## Method
Extended E043 cognacy comparison with semantic domain analysis.
210 ABVD concepts classified into: Maritime (10), Nature (14), Body (8), Action (4).
6 target languages: Old Javanese, Balinese, Javanese, Tengger, Malagasy, Muna.

## Results — SUCCESS

### Domain-level peripheral advantage (Balinese − Javanese):
1. **Nature: +28.6%** (Bal 85.7% vs Jav 57.1%) — strongest conservation
2. **Maritime: +20.0%** (Bal 60.0% vs Jav 40.0%) — #2 most conserved
3. ALL domains: +15.4%
4. Body: +12.5%
5. Action: -25.0% (reversed — small N=4)

### Maritime deep dive:
- "sea" (laut): Balinese retains PMP cognate; Javanese REPLACED
- "salt" (garam): ONLY Malagasy retains PMP form — maritime time capsule
- "rain", "wind", "rope": broadly conserved across all varieties
- "fish": PMP cognate lost in ALL modern varieties
- Tengger maritime: 30% (vs 45.8% general) — caldera community loses sea words

### Key insight:
Maritime vocabulary is the 2nd most conserved domain in peripheries.
The pre-Hindu substrate was not just organic but maritime-organic.
Court-driven overwriting targeted nature + maritime vocabulary.
