Having established that the slametan intervals are uniquely Javanese (Section 2), that a pre-Indic cosmological substrate persists in the epigraphic record (Section 3), and that the structural framework of gradual death is pan-Austronesian while the specific numerical intervals are locally variable (Section 4), we now turn to the central question: what generated the specific numbers 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000?

We propose that the slametan intervals correspond to observable stages of human decomposition in the acidic volcanic soils (*andosols*) characteristic of the Javanese landscape, and that the sequence represents a culturally encoded taphonomic calendar.

## 5.1 Decomposition in tropical volcanic soil

The environmental conditions of lowland Java — mean annual temperature of 26--28°C, relative humidity above 70%, annual rainfall exceeding 2,000 mm, and volcanic ash soils with pH 4.5--5.5 (ISRIC SoilGrids v2.0) — create a distinctive decomposition regime. Forensic taphonomy studies from comparable tropical environments provide the following stage-duration estimates for shallow-buried remains.

The fresh stage, during which autolysis begins and livor mortis becomes fixed, typically concludes within 1--3 days in tropical conditions (Rodriguez and Bass 1985; Galloway 1997). The bloat stage, characterised by putrefactive gas accumulation, skin marbling, and peak odour production, reaches its maximum intensity between days 3 and 7 in buried tropical remains (Galloway et al. 1989; Vass 2001). Active decay, during which soft tissue is consumed by microbial action and soil fauna, progresses rapidly in warm, moist, acidic soil; studies using accumulated degree-days (ADD) predict that at a mean temperature of 28°C, 40 days corresponds to approximately 1,120 ADD, a value consistently associated with advanced decay and the near-complete loss of soft tissue in the forensic literature (Megyesi et al. 2005). By 100 days (~2,800 ADD), skeletonisation is typically well advanced in tropical burial conditions, with only residual cartilage, ligaments, and desiccated tissue remaining (Haglund and Sorg 1997, 2002).

The critical variable for the 1000-day endpoint is bone preservation in acidic soil. Hydroxyapatite (Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂), the mineral component constituting approximately 70% of bone by weight, is chemically stable above pH 6.0 but undergoes progressive dissolution below this threshold (Nielsen-Marsh and Hedges 2000). In volcanic andosols at pH 4.5--5.5, bone mineral dissolution is actively promoted. Oghenemavwe et al. (2022) demonstrated measurable reduction in Haversian canal area in bone sections buried in soil at pH 2.98 after just six weeks. Archaeological case studies from sites with comparable soil acidity — Star Carr, England (pH 2--4; Milner et al. 2016) and Sutton Hoo, England (pH ~4; Bruce-Mitford 1975) — document near-complete dissolution of bone mineral within years to decades, leaving only collagen shadows or sand silhouettes. Kemp (2016) reports a forensic case in which "high soil acidity [was] associated with near complete mineral dissolution of recently buried human remains."

At 1000 days (approximately 2.7 years), significant bone degradation is taphonomically plausible in Javanese volcanic soil. While controlled experiments spanning this duration in andosol are lacking — this represents a genuine research gap — the convergence of experimental short-term data, archaeological case studies, and the known chemistry of hydroxyapatite dissolution supports the inference that skeletal remains would be substantially degraded, approaching the condition described by Geertz (1960: 72) as "fully decayed to dust."

## 5.2 The interval mapping: Javanese belief meets forensic science

The correspondence between slametan intervals and decomposition stages is not merely inferred from Western forensic science. Javanese tradition itself preserves explicit descriptions of the body's physical state at each ceremonial milestone. The concept of *kasampurnan* (perfection or completion) organises the sequence: each slametan marks the completion of decomposition for specific body components (Kalimullah 2016; cf. Soemodidjojo 1980, chapters 332--334).

Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim (2020: 437), drawing on field interviews in Mojokerto, East Java, document that the 40-day *matangpuluh dina* ceremony is understood as the point at which the body's "blood, flesh, marrow, innards, nails, hair, bones, and muscle" (*darah, daging, sungsum, jeroan, kuku, rambut, tulang, dan otot*) undergo *penyempurnaan* (perfection/completion). This is not a vague spiritual metaphor but a systematic inventory of tissue types, consistent with the forensic prediction for advanced decay at approximately 1,120 ADD.

At the second anniversary (*mendhak pindo*, approximately 730 days), two independent academic sources record the belief that "the body has almost dissolved; only bones remain" (*jenazah sudah hampir luluh, yang tersisa hanya tulang saja*) (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 439; Sholihah et al. 2023: 370). This corresponds precisely to the forensic expectation for a burial in acidic volcanic soil: soft tissue fully decomposed, bone surface degradation underway but skeleton still partially recognisable.

At 1000 days (*nyewu*), the Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna describes the goal as "the perfection of the human body, including its smell and taste, such that the body has become fully one with the earth" (*kesempurnaan jasad manusia, termasuk bau dan rasanya; jasad menyatu sepenuhnya dengan tanah*) (Kalimullah 2016, citing Soemodidjojo 1980). Geertz (1960: 72) independently records the same belief: at 1000 days, "the body has completely decayed to dust." Ki Sabdalangit adds: "from 1000 days the deceased enters the eternal dimension" (cited in Moussons journal, openedition.org/moussons/4302).

The ordering of these correspondences is not coincidental. The slametan sequence tracks the progressive loss of body components in the exact order predicted by forensic taphonomy: vital processes → skin → soft tissue → skeleton → bone → earth. The Javanese tradition names this sequence with a specificity that implies observational knowledge rather than doctrinal invention.

## 5.3 The pH--timing prediction

If the slametan intervals reflect empirical observation of decomposition in volcanic soil, a testable prediction follows: communities living in environments with different soil chemistry should exhibit different mortuary interval timing. Specifically, communities in limestone or coral-derived alkaline soils (pH 7--8), where bone mineral is chemically stable for centuries to millennia, should either lack a fixed terminal ceremony analogous to *nyewu* or should set this ceremony at a substantially later date.

Conversely, communities in highly acidic environments (peat swamps, laterite highlands) might be expected to observe shorter or more compressed mortuary sequences. The Berawan of Borneo (soil pH 4.8), who famously store corpses in above-ground containers to observe decomposition directly before performing secondary burial (Metcalf 1982; Metcalf and Huntington 1991), represent a particularly intriguing case: their practice of monitoring the decomposition process as a ritual guide is precisely the behaviour the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis predicts.

Testing this prediction rigorously would require compiling ethnographic data on specific mortuary interval timing for diverse Austronesian communities and cross-referencing these with local soil pH data — a task that exceeds the scope of this paper but that the combination of existing databases (Pulotu for beliefs, ISRIC SoilGrids for soil chemistry) makes feasible as a future research programme.

## 5.4 Caveats

We do not claim exact correspondence between slametan intervals and decomposition timelines. Decomposition rates are highly variable, influenced by body mass, clothing, burial depth, seasonal temperature fluctuation, soil moisture, and scavenger access (Haglund and Sorg 2002; Carter and Tibbett 2008). No published decomposition study has been conducted specifically in Javanese andosol — a research gap that future forensic taphonomy work in tropical volcanic environments should address.

Our claim is more limited: that the *ordering* of the intervals and their *approximate magnitude* are consistent with what would be expected from generations of empirical observation of burial decomposition in acidic volcanic soil. The Javanese tradition does not require scientific instruments to register these stages — the progression from odour (bloat) through visible decay to bare bone to dissolution into earth is observable to any community that practises shallow burial and periodic grave visitation.
