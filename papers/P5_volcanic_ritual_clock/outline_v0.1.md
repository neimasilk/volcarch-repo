## ABSTRACT (~200 words)

The Javanese slametan death ritual follows a sequence of commemorations at 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days after death. We demonstrate three findings: (1) this complete numerical sequence has no source in Hindu, Buddhist, or Islamic scripture and is uniquely Javanese; (2) the intervals correspond systematically to forensic decomposition stages in acidic volcanic soil (pH 4.5–5.5), with the terminal 1000-day mark matching the point at which skeletal remains degrade to unrecognizable fragments; (3) the underlying belief structure — death as gradual process, post-mortem ritual efficacy — is pan-Austronesian, shared by at least 30 of 137 cultures in the Pulotu database, including geographically separated populations in Madagascar, Sulawesi, the Philippines, and Melanesia. We propose the "Volcanic Ritual Clock" hypothesis: that Javanese mortuary timing was empirically calibrated to observable decomposition in volcanic landscapes, and that cross-Austronesian variation in mortuary intervals may correlate with local soil chemistry and decomposition rates. This connects ethnographic practice to geological process, linking the 1000-day ritual timescale to the 1000-year geological timescale at which volcanic sedimentation buries archaeological sites — the same environment that calibrates the ritual clock also destroys the evidence of the people who kept it.

## 1. INTRODUCTION (~1,200 words)

### 1.1 The Problem
- Death rituals are among the most conservative cultural practices — yet their material correlates (bodies, offerings, grave goods) are subject to taphonomic destruction
- In volcanic landscapes, two processes converge: (a) acidic soil accelerates decomposition, (b) tephra sedimentation buries sites
- These processes operate at different timescales but share a common driver: volcanism
- **Question:** Could mortuary ritual timing reflect empirical observation of decomposition rates?

### 1.2 The Slametan Cycle
- Brief ethnographic introduction: Javanese slametan as communal ritual meal
- The death sequence: nelung dina (3), mitung dina (7), matang puluh (40), nyatus (100), mendhak (1yr, 2yr), nyewu (1000)
- Geertz (1960): "at 1000 days the body has completely decayed to dust"
- Ki Sabdalangit: "from 1000 days the deceased enters the eternal dimension"
- **The key observation:** The ritual endpoint is defined by a physical process (complete decomposition), not a theological doctrine

### 1.3 Prior Work
- Robert Hertz (1907): physical decomposition parallels social death — body's decay = soul's transition
- Bloch & Parry (1982): cross-cultural patterns in death ritual timing
- Beatty (1999): multivocal interpretation of Javanese religion
- Metcalf & Huntington (1991): secondary burial as universal Austronesian trait
- **Gap:** No previous study has tested whether mortuary interval timing correlates with LOCAL decomposition rates determined by soil chemistry

### 1.4 This Paper's Contribution
- Framing the H-TOM synthesis: ritual timescale (1000 days) + geological timescale (1000 years)
- Three lines of evidence: epigraphic (DHARMA), comparative (Pulotu), taphonomic (forensic science + ISRIC soil data)

## 2. SOURCE EXCLUSION: The Slametan Sequence Is Uniquely Javanese (~1,500 words)

### 2.1 Hindu Sources
- Shraddha follows 10–13 day cycle (sapindikarana on day 12–13)
- Annual Pitru Paksha observances
- No 40, 100, or 1000-day interval in any Hindu text
- Table: Hindu mortuary intervals vs. Javanese slametan intervals

### 2.2 Buddhist Sources
- Bardo: 49 days (7 × 7), then rebirth
- 100-day mourning in East Asian Buddhism (possible Confucian overlay)
- No 40-day or 1000-day interval
- Partial overlap (7-day unit only) ≠ source for full sequence

### 2.3 Islamic Sources
- Orthodox mourning: 3 days (widows: 4 months 10 days)
- 3-7-40-100-1000 day commemorations explicitly classified as *bid'ah* (innovation) by Islamic jurisprudence
- Indonesian Islamic scholars recognize these as *adat* (local custom), not *syariat* (religious law)
- Reference: Al-Jami'ah journal, "Revisiting the Javanese Muslim Slametan"

### 2.4 Verdict
- The complete 3-7-40-100-1000 sequence cannot be derived from any single imported religious tradition
- It is a Javanese synthesis with its own internal logic
- **Key question:** If not imported, what generated these specific numbers?

## 3. EPIGRAPHIC EVIDENCE: Pre-Indic Substrate in Old Javanese Inscriptions (~1,500 words)

### 3.1 Corpus and Method
- DHARMA ERC corpus: 268 inscriptions (CC-BY 4.0)
- Classification: Old Javanese (155), Sanskrit (65), Old Sundanese (14), Old Malay (13)
- Keyword ontology: 25 ritual/cosmological terms classified as indic / pre_indic / ambiguous
- Method: systematic screening + frequency analysis

### 3.2 Results
- Pre-Indic elements detected in 126/268 (47%) inscriptions
- **hyaṁ/hyang** (PMP *qiang "spirit/divine"): 116/268 (43%)
  - Proto-Malayo-Polynesian reconstruction → pre-dates Indianization
  - Present in OJ (66%), Old Sundanese (43%), Old Malay (46%), but only 6% of Sanskrit-only texts
  - Persists universally alongside imported deva/devata terminology
- **maṅhuri** ("ancestor return"): 28/268 (10%)
  - No Sanskrit etymon
  - Cross-Austronesian parallels: Malagasy famadihana (bone-turning), Torajan ma'nene
- **wuku** (210-day indigenous calendar): 5/268 (2%)
  - Co-exists with imported Śaka calendar in the same inscriptions
  - Found even in Old Malay inscription from Sumatra (Dharmasraya A)
- **kabuyutan** (ancestral sacred site): in Old Sundanese corpus (Kebantenan)

### 3.3 Interpretation
- The epigraphic record preserves a layer of indigenous cosmology beneath the Indic surface
- The slametan cycle, though not in prasasti (oral tradition), belongs to this same substrate
- The persistence of hyang alongside deva shows the indigenous system was not displaced but overlaid

## 4. CROSS-AUSTRONESIAN COMPARISON: The Mortuary Package (~1,500 words)

### 4.1 Pulotu Database
- 137 Austronesian cultures, standardized variables (Watts et al. 2015)
- Key variable Q10: "Do actions of others AFTER death affect afterlife?"
- Full mortuary package definition: Q4 ≥ 2 (deified ancestors) AND Q5 ≥ 2 (ancestral spirits) AND Q10 ≥ 1 (post-death ritual efficacy)

### 4.2 Results
- 30/137 cultures share the full mortuary package
- Geographic spread: Indonesia (Toraja, Karo Batak, Toba Batak, Roti, Ata Tana 'Ai, Laboya), Madagascar (Merina, Tanala), Philippines (Ifugao, Visayans, Kalinga, Subanun, Tinguian), Melanesia (Trobriand, Roviana, Tanna, Simbo, Berawan), Polynesia (Marquesas)
- **Key finding:** Southern Toraja has Q10 as "major focus" — consistent with elaborate Rambu Solo funeral and the belief that ancestors require ongoing ritual attention
- Merina + Toraja BOTH in the full-package group despite ~8,000 km geographic separation → supports common Austronesian origin

### 4.3 Structure vs. Numbers
- The STRUCTURE is pan-Austronesian: death as gradual process, multiple ritual interventions, soul journey parallels body decay
- The NUMBERS are locally calibrated:
  - Java: 3-7-40-100-1000 days
  - Madagascar: 3–7 year cycles (famadihana)
  - Toraja: weeks to years (variable by wealth/status)
  - Philippines: secondary burial timing varies
- **Prediction:** The specific numbers reflect local environmental observation → testable via soil data

## 5. TAPHONOMIC CALIBRATION: The 1000-Day Hypothesis (~1,500 words)

### 5.1 Forensic Decomposition in Volcanic Soil
- Literature: decomposition rates in tropical environments (Rodriguez & Bass 1985, Galloway et al. 1989, Manhein 1997)
- Key environmental variables: soil pH, temperature, moisture, microbial activity
- In volcanic soil (pH 4.5–5.5, T = 25–28°C, high moisture):
  - Fresh stage ends: ~day 3
  - Bloat stage: ~day 3–7
  - Active decay (soft tissue largely gone): ~day 20–40
  - Skeletonization: ~day 100–365
  - Significant bone degradation (acidic dissolution): ~day 500–1000
  - "Dust" (no recognizable skeleton): ~day 1000+
- **The slametan intervals map systematically to these stages**

### 5.2 The Interval Mapping — With Javanese Ethnographic Evidence
- Table: Javanese interval | Name | Javanese belief about body state | Taphonomic stage | Sources
  - 3 days / nelung dina / "Spirit still in the house" (roh masih terdapat di dalam rumah) / End of fresh stage / Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020; Rodriguez & Bass 1985
  - 7 days / mitung dina / "Spirit begins to leave house" (membuka genting/jendela for spirit) / Bloat stage peak / Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020; Galloway et al. 1989
  - 40 days / matang puluh / **Explicit body-part inventory: "darah, daging, sungsum, jeroan, kuku, rambut, tulang, dan otot" (blood, flesh, marrow, innards, nails, hair, bones, muscle)** must be "perfected" / Soft tissue largely gone / Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020:437; Manhein 1997
  - 100 days / nyatus / "Perfecting the physical body (badan wadhag)" / Skeletonization underway / Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020:437; Haglund & Sorg 1997
  - 730 days / mendhak pindo / **"Jenazah sudah hampir luluh, tinggal tulang saja" (body almost dissolved, only bones remain)** / Bone surface degradation / Sholihah et al. ICCL 2023:370; Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020:439; Nielsen-Marsh & Hedges 2000
  - 1000 days / nyewu / "Spirit will no longer return; fully left to face God" = Geertz: "body fully decayed to dust" / Complete bone degradation in acidic soil / Hendrajaya & Almu'tasim 2020:440; Geertz 1960
- **KEY: The body-state language is NOT metaphorical** — the 40-day ceremony names specific tissue types. The 2-year mark explicitly states "only bones remain." These are observational descriptions.
- Emphasis: this is NOT a claim of exact timing. Decomposition rates vary. The claim is that the ORDERING and APPROXIMATE TIMING are consistent with empirical observation in volcanic soil environments.

### 5.3 The pH–Timing Prediction
- Limestone regions (pH 7–8): bone preserved for centuries → longer mortuary intervals expected
- Volcanic regions (pH 4.5–5.5): bone degrades within years → shorter/more specified intervals expected
- Testable with ISRIC SoilGrids data × Pulotu culture locations
- Preliminary results: [soil pH cross-reference data, if available by draft time]

### 5.4 Malagasy Comparison
- Famadihana timing (3–7 years) in laterite soil (pH 4.5–5.5, highlands)
- Laterite decomposes bone slower than volcanic tephra (different mineral composition)
- Prediction: famadihana interval is calibrated to highland Madagascar decomposition rate → testable

## 6. THE H-TOM SYNTHESIS: Two Timescales (~1,000 words)

### 6.1 Ritual Timescale (1000 days)
- Slametan calendar calibrated to body decomposition in volcanic soil
- Ritual marks the transition: when body becomes "dust," soul is complete → nyewu
- This is a cultural encoding of taphonomic observation

### 6.2 Geological Timescale (1000 years)
- Volcanic sedimentation rate in East Java: ~3.6–3.7 mm/yr (P1 calibration, P9 borehole data)
- At this rate: after 1000 years, sites buried ~3.6 m deep (beyond casual discovery)
- The same acidic tephra that accelerates body decomposition also buries the archaeological sites

### 6.3 The Paradox
- The volcanic environment that calibrates the ritual clock ALSO destroys the archaeological evidence of the people who kept that clock
- This is the H-TOM (Hypothetical Taphonomic Overlay Model) synthesis:
  - Ritual timing → body decomposes → grave goods dissolve → archaeological signal lost
  - Landscape burial → sites under meters of tephra → archaeological invisibility
  - **The living culture records (in ritual) the very process that erases its material past**
- Figure: two-timescale diagram showing 1000 days (ritual) and 1000 years (geological) as parallel consequences of volcanism

### 6.4 Implications for Archaeological Interpretation
- Open-air sites in volcanic Java are systematically underrepresented (P1, P2, P7)
- Even when sites are located, organic preservation is poor due to soil acidity
- The slametan cycle is a "living fossil" — cultural evidence of the taphonomic process
- Cf. how Aboriginal Australian fire practices encode ecological knowledge → slametan encodes taphonomic knowledge

## 7. DISCUSSION (~1,000 words)

### 7.1 Limitations
- Decomposition rates cited from general forensic literature, not site-specific Javanese cemetery data
- Slametan timing may have been modified over centuries (no way to date the "original" intervals)
- Pulotu data is coded at culture level, not community level — variation within cultures is lost
- ISRIC soil data is modern; historic soil pH may differ (but volcanic soil pH is relatively stable)
- Risk of "just-so" story: correlation between intervals and decomposition stages could be coincidence

### 7.2 Falsification Criteria
- If cultures in high-pH (limestone) areas show the SAME mortuary timing as volcanic areas → hypothesis weakened
- If Primbon contains explicit theological (non-observational) rationale for interval choices → hypothesis modified
- If forensic data from Javanese cemeteries shows radically different decomposition rates → hypothesis fails

### 7.3 Broader Significance
- Connects ethnographic practice to geoarchaeological process for the first time
- Suggests that mortuary rituals may contain encoded environmental information
- Opens new research direction: "taphonomic ethnography" — using living ritual practices to infer burial conditions
- Implications for forensic archaeology in volcanic regions

---

## 8. CONCLUSION (~500 words)

- Three findings: (1) uniquely Javanese origin, (2) taphonomic calibration, (3) pan-Austronesian structure
- The slametan cycle is a cultural calendar calibrated to volcanic decomposition
- Two timescales — 1000 days (body) and 1000 years (landscape) — linked by the same volcanic driver
- This is not a reductionist argument: we do not claim ritual is "nothing but" decomposition observation. Rather, the physical process PROVIDED the empirical scaffold upon which rich cosmological meaning was built.
- Future work: (a) field validation with Javanese cemetery soil data, (b) Primbon primary source analysis, (c) expanded Pulotu × soil chemistry correlation, (d) Malagasy famadihana timing prediction test
