We submit the above manuscript for consideration as a Research Article in the *Journal of Southeast Asian Studies*.

**Summary.** The paper proposes the Peripheral Conservatism Framework (PCF), a systematic methodology for recovering pre-Hindu cultural substrates in Nusantara from living peripheral communities rather than from archaeological excavation at overwritten centres. Three independent evidence channels --- lexical conservation (ABVD cognacy analysis), mortuary ritual structure (Trunyan, Toraja, famadihana), and botanical burial practice (the Plumeria/Canarium substitution chain) --- converge on a consistent pattern: peripheral communities retain archaic Austronesian elements that centres have lost through successive overwriting by Indianization, Islamization, and colonialism. Madagascar provides a dateable *c.* 1200 CE calibration point. The Indianization of Java is shown to have been a wave (peak ninth century, declining thereafter), not a permanent transformation.

**Scope and contribution.** The manuscript is interdisciplinary, integrating historical linguistics, epigraphy, ethnobotany, and computational archaeology. It speaks directly to JSEAS's remit in the humanities and social sciences of Southeast Asia, with a focus on Indonesia (Java, Bali, East Java, Madagascar as Austronesian diaspora). The PCF offers a novel methodological contribution to the study of pre-Hindu Nusantara --- a period that remains poorly understood precisely because the standard evidentiary channels (archaeology, epigraphy) are systematically biased against organic material cultures in volcanic environments.
