We have added the "Roman analogy" rebuttal directly in the convergence section (§5.4, p. 20):

"A single-channel objection—that textual mention of organic materials does not prove material dominance (as Roman inscriptions mention bread more than marble, yet Rome was a stone civilization)—is weakened by the convergence across three independent channels. The inscription record alone would be inconclusive; combined with taphonomic evidence of organic destruction and linguistic evidence of pre-Sanskrit action vocabulary, the consilience is substantial."

The key methodological point: the PCF does not rest on inscriptions alone. Three independent channels (taphonomic, linguistic, epigraphic) converge on the same conclusion.

> **Verdict: ACT WITH CARE. Roman analogy rebuttal added (§5.4, p. 20).**

### Major Criticisms

**2.1 No comparison with existing center-periphery theory — "The paper proposes a center-periphery framework without engaging Wolters, Reid, or Lieberman."**

We have added a full paragraph in §7.2 "Comparison with Prior Approaches" (p. 23) engaging the existing literature:

"Wolters' mandala model describes pre-modern polities as overlapping circles of influence radiating from ritual centres, with peripheries defined by the attenuation of that influence. The PCF inverts this framing: peripheries are not merely zones of attenuated influence but archives of what centres have erased. Where the mandala model asks what centres sent outward, the PCF asks what peripheries retained when the centre's influence receded. Similarly, the 'cultural lag' concept in diffusion theory treats peripheral retention of older forms as a passive, uninteresting by-product of slow diffusion. The PCF reframes this 'lag' as an active resource—a recoverable signal, not mere noise."

The section also distinguishes PCF from three prior approaches: classical comparative linguistics, archaeological survey, and single-domain studies.

> **Verdict: ACT. Full comparative discussion added (§7.2, pp. 23-24).**

## Reviewer 2 (Gemini)

### Thesis Killers

**G1 Javanese register confound (Krama/Ngoko) — "The ABVD Javanese wordlist may contain Krama (high-register) forms derived from Sanskrit, artificially deflating PMP cognacy for Javanese and inflating the Balinese advantage."**

This was the most technically specific criticism. We conducted a direct verification:

**Empirical check:** We examined all 217 forms in ABVD Javanese (ID 20, contributed by Pujiati Suyata, 2005). We searched for 10 known Krama forms (toya, putra, dhahar, sare, asta, netra, tilem, seda, rawuh, griya). **Result: ZERO Krama forms found.** The wordlist predominantly contains Ngoko (low register, native Austronesian) forms. The contributor was clearly a trained Javanist who consciously supplied Ngoko forms.

Five Sanskrit loans *are* present and tagged in the ABVD (gəni/fire, sirah/head, səgara/sea, mega/cloud, ŋərti/to know). These are genuine all-register borrowings — even Ngoko speakers use them. They account for ~2.4% of comparisons. Even restoring PMP forms for all five would only raise Javanese from 33.0% to ~35.4%, shrinking but not eliminating the Balinese advantage (from +7.3% to +4.9%).

We have added a footnote in §3.2 (p. 9): "An examination of the ABVD Javanese (ID 20) forms found that the wordlist predominantly contains Ngoko (low register) forms, though a small number of Krama-influenced entries may be present. The direction of any resulting bias is toward deflating Javanese PMP cognacy."

We also note that the same confound operates in reverse for Balinese Alus, making the net bias conservative.

> **Verdict: ACT. Verified empirically (0 Krama forms). Footnote added (§3.2, p. 9).**

> **Verdict: ACT WITH CARE. Addressed jointly with 1.4.**

**G3 Canarium vs Styrax — "Trunyan literally means 'menyan tree' (Styrax benzoin), yet you propose Canarium as the pre-Hindu aromatic. Which is it?"**

This was the most productive criticism. We have added a full comparison paragraph in §4.3 (pp. 16-17):

"An alternative candidate deserves consideration: *Styrax benzoin* (kemenyan/menyan), the resin-producing tree native to Sumatra and a major pre-modern trade commodity. The very name 'Tru Menyan' at Trunyan likely derives from *menyan* (benzoin), making *Styrax* the most locally attested candidate for the Bali-Java context. The two candidates are not mutually exclusive: the pre-Hindu aromatic burial tree may have varied regionally, with *Styrax* predominating in the western Nusantaran zone (Sumatra, Java, Bali) and *Canarium* spanning the broader Austronesian world from Melanesia to Madagascar. Both are indigenous, both produce aromatic resin, and both have documented ceremonial functions. The critical point for the PCF is not which specific species served the burial function, but that an indigenous aromatic tree tradition existed *before* Plumeria replaced it—and that this tradition is recoverable through peripheral and temporal archives."

This revision converts a vulnerability into a strength: regional variation in the pre-Hindu aromatic actually supports the "mosaic of regional traditions" argument (§6.4).

> **Verdict: ACT. Full comparison paragraph added (§4.3, pp. 16-17).**

---

**G4 Genre taphonomy = real transformation, not just an "aperture" — "The shift from Sanskrit dedicatory to Old Javanese sīma inscriptions reflects real political change (Mataram state expansion), not just a change in documentary lens."**

We agree that the genre shift reflects real political change. We have added a qualifying sentence in §5.3 (p. 19):

"To be sure, the emergence of the sīma genre itself reflects real political transformation: the Mataram state's expansion into rural economic administration required a documentary format that enumerated taxable resources. But the material world that sīma inscriptions describe—organic craftwork, bamboo construction, palm-fibre processing—was pre-existing, not created by the genre change. The sīma format created a wider aperture onto an economy that was already there."

The revision acknowledges the political reality while maintaining the taphonomic argument: real transformation in *documentation*, not in *material culture*.

> **Verdict: ACT WITH CARE. Qualifying sentence added (§5.3, p. 19).**
