## 6. The H-TOM Synthesis: Two Timescales of Volcanic Erasure

The preceding sections have established three propositions: that the slametan death sequence is indigenous to Java (Section 2), that its structural framework is pan-Austronesian while its specific numerical intervals are locally variable (Section 4), and that the Javanese intervals correspond systematically to forensic decomposition stages in acidic volcanic soil (Section 5). We now draw these threads together through the Hypothetical Taphonomic Overlay Model (H-TOM), which identifies two distinct timescales — one ritual, one geological — that are both products of the same volcanic environment.

### 6.1 The ritual timescale: 1000 days

The slametan death cycle operates on a timescale of approximately 2.7 years from burial to final ceremony. Across this interval, each ritual marks the progressive physical disappearance of the body. At 40 days, the ceremony addresses specific tissue types — blood, flesh, marrow, innards, nails, hair, bones, and muscle (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 437) — a systematic inventory that corresponds to the forensic expectation for advanced soft-tissue decay in tropical burial. At 730 days, two independent sources record the belief that "the body has almost dissolved; only bones remain" (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 439; Sholihah et al. 2023: 370). At 1000 days, the Primbon describes "the perfection of the human body, including its smell and taste — the body has become fully one with the earth" (Kalimullah 2016, citing Soemodidjojo 1980), and Geertz (1960: 72) independently records the same belief: at *nyewu*, the body has "completely decayed to dust."

The organising principle is *kasampurnan* — perfection through completion. But the content of this perfection is physical, not theological. The Javanese tradition does not speak of the soul reaching paradise at 1000 days because a scripture prescribes it; it speaks of the body returning to earth because that is what bodies do in volcanic soil. The ritual marks the empirical observation. When bone mineral dissolves in andosol at pH 4.5--5.5 — a process well-attested in forensic taphonomy (Nielsen-Marsh and Hedges 2000; Oghenemavwe et al. 2022) — the skeleton genuinely approaches disappearance within the timeframe the slametan specifies. The soul's departure tracks the body's dissolution, and the community's ritual calendar is calibrated to this observable process.

This is a cultural encoding of taphonomic knowledge. The slametan does not require its practitioners to possess scientific vocabulary; it requires only that communities practising shallow burial in acidic volcanic soil, with institutionalised grave visitation during *bulan Ruwah* (Soemodidjojo 1980: no. 334), accumulated generations of observational knowledge about what happens to buried remains. The intervals are not arbitrary. They are empirical.

### 6.2 The geological timescale: 1000 years

The same volcanic processes that accelerate body decomposition also operate at a geological timescale. Volcanic sedimentation in East Java proceeds at a rate of approximately 3.6 mm/yr, as calibrated by the Dwarapala of Singosari — a 3.7-metre stone guardian statue photographed half-buried in 1860 and now interred to shoulder height, yielding a sedimentation rate of 3.6 mm/yr over 510 years (Amien 2026a). This rate is independently corroborated by the expanding dataset of 25 buried archaeological sites across Java (Amien 2026c), which yields a distal mean of approximately 3.7 mm/yr for sites located more than 10 km from their source volcano.

At 3.6 mm/yr, one millennium of volcanic sedimentation buries the landscape surface by approximately 3.6 metres — a depth well beyond casual discovery, below the reach of ploughing, foundation-digging, or erosional exposure. This is not a theoretical projection: Candi Sambisari, a ninth-century Hindu temple 28 km from Merapi, was discovered in 1966 under 5--6 metres of volcanic deposits (Newhall et al. 2000). Candi Kedulan, at the same distance, lies beneath 7--8 metres of sediment with 14 distinguishable stratigraphic layers (Tjahjono 2003). Candi Pendem — whose name literally means "buried temple" — was found under 4 metres of lahar deposit near Borobudur (Tjahjono 2003). These are not isolated cases. The dataset now includes temples at Morangan (3.5 m), Kadisoka (3 m), Kimpulan (2 m), Pleret (2.5 m), and the Liangan Buddhist settlement (4 m under pyroclastic surge), each independently confirming that open-air sites in volcanic Java are progressively and systematically buried.

Near volcanic centres, the rates are far higher: the Kelud stratigraphic record documents 32 metres of volcanic deposits in 1,300 years, yielding approximately 24.6 mm/yr at the vent (de Belizal et al. 2013). A clear burial-rate gradient operates: near-vent rates exceed 10 mm/yr, while distal rates stabilise around 3.6--4.5 mm/yr (Amien 2026c). The implication is that the depth of burial is predictable as a function of distance from the volcanic source and time elapsed.

### 6.3 The paradox: the clock erases its own evidence

The two timescales — 1000 days and 1000 years — are parallel consequences of the same volcanic driver. The acidic tephra that weathers into andosol dissolves bone mineral within years, accelerating body decomposition and thereby calibrating the slametan intervals. The same tephra, accumulating as sediment, buries the graves, the temples, the settlements, and the entire material record of the civilisation that produced the ritual. This is the H-TOM paradox:

> *The volcanic environment that calibrates the ritual clock also destroys the archaeological evidence of the people who kept that clock.*

The paradox operates at every level. At the level of the individual burial, acidic soil dissolves grave goods, offerings, organic remains, and eventually bone, leaving little for future excavation. At the level of the settlement, tephra sedimentation buries living surfaces under metres of volcanic material, removing them from the archaeological survey record. At the level of the regional dataset, the cumulative effect produces the pattern documented in Papers 1 and 7: a systematic underrepresentation of open-air sites in volcanic landscapes, with deep-time evidence surviving only in limestone caves and karst shelters where neither soil acidity nor sedimentation applies (Amien 2026a, 2026b).

The slametan cycle is thus a peculiar kind of evidence: a *living record* of the very taphonomic process that destroys the *material record*. The ritual preserves, in cultural memory, knowledge of decomposition rates that would otherwise be inaccessible to archaeology. In Robert Hertz's (1907) terms, the "wet" phase of decomposition (from burial through soft-tissue loss) and the "dry" phase (skeletonisation through bone dissolution) are tracked step by step in the ritual calendar — a cultural parallel to the stratigraphic record that volcanic sedimentation is simultaneously building and burying.

### 6.4 Implications: the slametan as taphonomic fossil

The analogy with other forms of encoded environmental knowledge is instructive. Aboriginal Australian fire management practices — systematic burning calibrated to seasonal cycles, soil moisture, and vegetation recovery — encode ecological knowledge accumulated over millennia, knowledge that Western science has only recently begun to formalise (Gammage 2011; Pascoe 2014). The knowledge is embedded in practice, not in written theory; its transmission is cultural, not academic; and its validity was long dismissed by colonial observers who lacked the framework to recognise empirical observation in ritual form.

We suggest that the slametan death cycle operates analogously: it encodes taphonomic knowledge — knowledge of what happens to buried bodies in specific soil conditions over specific time periods — in ritual practice. Like Aboriginal fire regimes, this knowledge was generated through long-term observation, transmitted through cultural institutions, and expressed in a form that does not resemble Western scientific discourse but is no less empirically grounded.

For archaeological interpretation, this has a direct consequence. Open-air sites in volcanic Java are not merely underrepresented; they are *predictably* absent from the surface record. The slametan cycle tells us that Javanese communities knew their dead would disappear into the earth within approximately three years. The geological record tells us that their settlements would follow within approximately a millennium. The cultural knowledge and the geological process are two expressions of a single volcanic reality — and the living ritual is, in a strict sense, a "taphonomic fossil": a surviving trace of the process that otherwise leaves no trace at all.

Cross-referencing with specific buried sites makes this concrete. Candi Sambisari, buried 6.5 metres deep, was constructed in the ninth century — approximately 1,100 years before its accidental discovery. At 3.6 mm/yr, this predicts a burial depth of approximately 4.0 metres; the greater observed depth reflects episodic lahar deposition in addition to background sedimentation. Candi Kedulan, at 4.2--8.0 metres, follows the same pattern. These are temples — stone structures designed for permanence. The organic material culture that surrounded them — the wooden houses, the textile offerings, the buried dead — has long since been dissolved by the same acidic soil that the slametan tracks.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the strength of the argument presented here. The most consequential is the absence of site-specific forensic decomposition data from Javanese volcanic soil. The taphonomic calibration in Section 5 draws on forensic studies conducted in temperate environments (Rodriguez and Bass 1985; Galloway et al. 1989), tropical surface contexts (Archer 2004), and experimental bone dissolution at various pH values (Oghenemavwe et al. 2022; Nielsen-Marsh and Hedges 2000). No published study has measured decomposition rates in Indonesian andosol. While the convergence of evidence from multiple forensic and archaeological contexts supports the plausibility of the proposed timeline, the absence of direct experimental confirmation means that the interval-to-stage mapping remains inferential rather than demonstrated.

Second, the antiquity and stability of the slametan intervals cannot be independently verified. The earliest documentary evidence for the complete 3--7--40--100--1000 sequence comes from the Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna, a compendium whose contents are traditionally attributed to KPH Danurejo VI but whose textual history is not securely dated (Kalimullah 2016). Aizid (2015: 149) asserts that the death slametan pre-dates Hindu-Buddhist contact, but this claim is based on cultural inference rather than on textual or archaeological evidence. It is possible that the intervals have been modified over centuries — by Hindu influence on the 7-day unit, by Buddhist influence on the 100-day mark, or by processes of rationalisation internal to Javanese culture — and that the "original" sequence, if one existed, differed from the modern form.

Third, the comparative data from the Pulotu database operate at the level of whole cultures rather than individual communities. The binary coding of variables such as Q10 ("do actions of others after death affect afterlife?") obscures substantial internal variation. Southern Torajan communities, for instance, vary dramatically in the duration and expense of funeral rites depending on social status and wealth (Wellenkamp 1988; Adams 2006); the Pulotu entry captures none of this variation. The cross-Austronesian comparison thus identifies broad structural parallels but cannot resolve the fine-grained timing differences that the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis predicts should correlate with local soil chemistry.

Fourth, the ISRIC SoilGrids data used for the soil pH cross-reference represent modern surface measurements. While andosol pH is geologically relatively stable — it is determined primarily by the mineralogy of the parent tephra rather than by recent land-use change (Shoji et al. 1993) — historical soil conditions at any specific burial site may have differed from current values, particularly in areas subject to intensive wet-rice cultivation, liming, or deforestation.

Finally, and most fundamentally, there is a risk that the argument constitutes a "just-so story": a post hoc narrative that imposes causal coherence on what may be coincidental correspondence. The slametan intervals are not the only possible set of numbers that could be mapped onto decomposition stages; a sufficiently motivated analyst could likely find correspondences with alternative decomposition timelines. This concern is not dismissible. It is addressed, but not fully resolved, by the falsification criteria below.

### 7.2 Falsification criteria

The Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis generates specific, testable predictions whose failure would weaken or invalidate the argument.

*Criterion 1: Environmental specificity.* If communities in high-pH (limestone or coral) environments exhibit the same mortuary interval timing as volcanic-soil communities, the hypothesis that intervals are calibrated to local decomposition rates would be substantially weakened. Preliminary cross-referencing with ISRIC soil data has shown that the pan-Austronesian *structure* (death as gradual process, post-mortem ritual efficacy) is independent of soil type — as predicted, since this structure is culturally inherited rather than environmentally determined. The critical test concerns the *specific numerical intervals*, not the structural framework. This test requires ethnographic data on mortuary timing for communities in contrasting soil environments, which the Pulotu database does not currently encode. A systematic compilation of interval data from 10--20 well-documented Austronesian cultures, cross-referenced with local soil pH, would constitute a rigorous test.

*Criterion 2: Indigenous rationale.* If the Primbon or other authoritative Javanese sources contain an explicit theological or cosmological rationale for the choice of specific intervals that is independent of observational knowledge of decomposition — for instance, a numerological system deriving 1000 from astrological calculation, or a narrative attributing the intervals to divine revelation — the hypothesis would require modification. The kasampurnan framework documented by Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim (2020), with its tissue-specific language, is currently more consistent with observational than with doctrinal origin, but this assessment rests on a limited number of sources and could be revised by more extensive Primbon scholarship.

*Criterion 3: Forensic disconfirmation.* If controlled decomposition studies in Javanese andosol demonstrate that bone mineral persists substantially beyond 1000 days — indicating that Geertz's "dust" description is observationally inaccurate for this soil type — the central calibration claim would fail. Conversely, if bone dissolution proceeds faster than 1000 days, the hypothesis would need to account for why the ritual interval overshoots the observed decomposition timeline. The critical experiment would involve monitored burial of animal bone proxies in andosol at representative pH values (4.5--5.5), with periodic sampling over a multi-year period.

### 7.3 Broader significance

If the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis survives empirical testing, it opens a research direction that we provisionally term "taphonomic ethnography": the systematic use of living ritual practices to infer burial conditions and decomposition regimes in specific environments. This approach inverts the usual relationship between ethnography and archaeology. Conventionally, ethnographic analogy is used to interpret archaeological evidence — to infer the function of artefacts, the meaning of spatial patterns, or the social organisation behind mortuary practices. Taphonomic ethnography works in the opposite direction: it uses the *content* of ritual practice to generate predictions about the *physical processes* operating on the material record, predictions that can then be tested against forensic and geoarchaeological data.

The broader implication is that mortuary rituals — and perhaps other categories of ritual practice — may contain encoded environmental information that is invisible to purely symbolic or theological analysis. The "perfection" (*kasampurnan*) vocabulary of the slametan appears, on first reading, to be a metaphysical concept; only when mapped against forensic taphonomy does its empirical substrate become visible. This suggests that other ritual systems, particularly those involving timed interventions relative to natural processes (harvest cycles, seasonal ceremonies, secondary burial practices), may reward similarly materialist analysis — not as a replacement for symbolic interpretation, but as a complement to it.

For Austronesian studies specifically, the distinction between inherited *structure* and locally calibrated *numbers* offers a framework for comparative mortuary analysis that is testable rather than merely typological. The Berawan of Borneo (soil pH 4.8), who monitor decomposition directly as a ritual guide (Metcalf 1982), the Merina of highland Madagascar (laterite soil, pH 4.7), whose famadihana timing spans years rather than days, and the Toraja of Sulawesi (volcanic highland, pH 5.0), whose funeral timing varies by wealth and status, all practise variants of the same pan-Austronesian mortuary structure. If their specific timing parameters correlate with local decomposition rates, the Volcanic Ritual Clock may prove to be not a uniquely Javanese phenomenon but an Austronesian one, with Java representing a particularly clear case because its volcanic soil conditions produce a decomposition timeline that falls within the range of direct human observation and ritual commemoration.

For forensic archaeology, the hypothesis suggests that volcanic regions in the tropics represent environments where conventional expectations for skeletal preservation must be substantially revised downward. Forensic cases involving burials in acidic volcanic soil should anticipate near-complete loss of skeletal material within years, not decades, and should factor soil pH into estimation of post-mortem interval — a variable that is not routinely considered in forensic protocols developed for temperate, circumneutral-pH environments.

## 8. Conclusion

This paper has advanced three findings. First, the complete slametan death sequence — 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days — cannot be derived from Hindu, Buddhist, or Islamic scripture. Multiple Indonesian scholars confirm its pre-Indic origin; Islamic jurisprudence explicitly classifies the practice as *bid'ah* (innovation), not *syariat* (law); and the Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna treats the intervals as indigenous calendrical computation using the Javanese *neptu/pasaran* system. The slametan sequence is Javanese.

Second, the intervals correspond systematically to the forensic stages of human decomposition in acidic volcanic soil. This is not merely an external analytical imposition: Javanese tradition itself preserves tissue-specific descriptions at each ceremonial milestone, from the 40-day body-part inventory (*darah, daging, sungsum, jeroan, kuku, rambut, tulang, dan otot*) to the 730-day observation that "only bones remain" to the 1000-day belief that the body has become "fully one with the earth." The organising principle of *kasampurnan* — perfection through the completion of decomposition — links the ritual calendar directly to taphonomic observation.

Third, the underlying structure — death as a gradual process requiring multiple ritual interventions, with the soul's journey paralleling the body's dissolution — is pan-Austronesian, shared by at least 30 of 137 cultures in the Pulotu database, including geographically distant populations in Madagascar, Sulawesi, the Philippines, and Melanesia. The specific *numbers*, however, are locally variable: Java counts in days, Madagascar in years, Toraja in seasons of variable duration. This distinction between shared structure and local calibration generates the prediction that specific mortuary timing should correlate with local decomposition conditions — a hypothesis that the combination of existing ethnographic and soil-chemistry databases makes testable.

These three findings converge in the H-TOM synthesis: two timescales, both driven by volcanism, operating in parallel. At the ritual timescale, the acidic volcanic soil of Java dissolves the buried body within approximately 1000 days, and the slametan calendar marks this dissolution step by step. At the geological timescale, volcanic sedimentation buries the landscape surface at approximately 3.6 mm/yr, placing sites beyond archaeological recovery within approximately 1000 years. The same environment calibrates the ritual and erases the evidence. The living culture records, in its most conservative and persistent institution — the death ritual — the very process that destroys its material past.

We do not advance this as a reductionist argument. To say that the slametan intervals were empirically calibrated to decomposition is not to say that the ritual is "nothing but" a taphonomic observation. Generations of Javanese communities built upon the empirical scaffold a rich cosmological architecture: the progressive departure of the soul, the duties of the living toward the dead, the social solidarity of communal feasting, the theological synthesis with successive religious traditions. The physical process provided the temporal framework; the culture provided the meaning. Our claim is that these two dimensions — the material and the symbolic — are not independent, and that attending to the material substrate of ritual timing reveals a form of environmental knowledge that has been transmitted across centuries without being recognised as such.

Future work should pursue four directions. First, controlled decomposition studies in Javanese andosol are needed to test whether bone mineral dissolution within approximately 1000 days is empirically confirmed or merely plausible. Second, the Primbon Betaljemur Adammakna requires systematic textual analysis to determine whether it contains additional explicit references to body state at specific intervals, or alternative rationales for the choice of numbers. Third, a cross-cultural dataset compiling specific mortuary interval timing for diverse Austronesian communities, cross-referenced with local soil pH from ISRIC SoilGrids, would permit the first quantitative test of the pH--timing prediction. Fourth, the Malagasy famadihana — operating on a years-long timescale in laterite soil of comparable acidity — offers a natural comparison case: if famadihana timing is calibrated to highland Madagascar decomposition rates, the Volcanic Ritual Clock hypothesis extends from a Javanese phenomenon to an Austronesian one.

The Javanese have a phrase: *sak madya, sak mesthine* — in moderation, as it should be. The slametan death cycle, understood as a taphonomic calendar, embodies this principle. It marks each stage of decomposition at approximately the interval it occurs, neither rushing the process nor denying it. It is, in the end, a form of honesty about what the volcanic earth does to the dead — and about what it does to the evidence of their lives.
