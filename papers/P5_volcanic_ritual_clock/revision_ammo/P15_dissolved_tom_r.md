## Abstract

Four major Javanese ritual complexes — ruwatan (protective exorcism), sedekah bumi (earth propitiation), nyadran (ancestral grave visitation), and labuhan (volcanic/maritime offering) — share a structural feature that has not been systematically examined: their operative mechanisms, timing systems, and propitiation targets are inconsistent with Hindu, Buddhist, Islamic, or Christian frameworks, while their surface terminology frequently employs Sanskrit or Arabic loanwords. We apply a source-exclusion methodology analogous to that used in the analysis of slametan mortuary intervals (Amien & Gunawan, submitted) to demonstrate that the structural core of each ritual belongs to a pre-Indic Austronesian substrate. The pattern that emerges is consistent across all four complexes: Indianization and Islamization modified *terminology* without modifying *structure*. We propose a general framework — the Terminological Overlay Model (TOM-R, ritual variant) — in which successive religious waves function as lexical relabelling operations on an underlying substrate that encodes Austronesian relations with landscape. Cross-Austronesian comparison using the Pulotu database (N=137 cultures) demonstrates that the structural elements are distributed across the Austronesian world independent of Hindu-Buddhist or Islamic contact. We discuss implications for the interpretation of the Javanese epigraphic record and for the broader VOLCARCH hypothesis that pre-4th century archaeological invisibility in Nusantara reflects taphonomic loss rather than genuine cultural absence.

**Keywords:** Javanese ritual; pre-Indic substrate; Austronesian religion; ruwatan; sedekah bumi; nyadran; labuhan; source exclusion; Pulotu database; VOLCARCH

## 1. Introduction

The Javanese ritual landscape presents a persistent interpretive problem. Four major ritual complexes — ruwatan, sedekah bumi, nyadran, and labuhan — are classified in popular and scholarly discourse as Hindu-Buddhist survivals, Islamic syncretism, or some blend of both. Yet each contains elements that neither Hindu-Buddhist nor Islamic frameworks can accommodate: mechanisms of ritual action that contradict orthodox theology, timing systems derived from non-Hindu and non-Islamic calendars, and relationships with landscape entities that have no canonical counterpart in the imported traditions.

The problem is not unique to these four complexes. Paper 5 of this series (Amien & Gunawan, submitted to *BKI*) demonstrated that the Javanese slametan mortuary interval sequence — commemorations at 3, 7, 40, 100, and 1000 days — cannot be derived from any of the four successive religious waves (Hindu, Buddhist, Islamic, Christian) that shaped Javanese culture from the 4th century CE onward. The intervals, it was argued, reflect empirical calibration to the pace of decomposition in volcanic Andosol, and represent a pre-Indic Austronesian mortuary tradition that survived Indianization and Islamization by being structurally invisible to the incoming systems — they were not *replaced* because they were not recognised as a competing religious claim.

The present paper extends this analysis to four additional ritual complexes. Our central hypothesis is that the pattern identified in the slametan case is general: *Indianization and Islamization in Java functioned primarily as terminological overlay operations, relabelling pre-Indic structural elements with Sanskrit or Arabic vocabulary without displacing the underlying mechanisms.*

This hypothesis has significant implications. If true, it means that the full extent of pre-Indic Javanese culture is larger than the archaeological and textual records suggest — a conclusion consistent with the broader VOLCARCH argument that the thinness of the pre-4th century archaeological record in Nusantara reflects taphonomic loss (volcanic burial, coastal submersion) rather than genuine cultural absence. Living ritual practice may preserve structural evidence of that lost world.

## 2. Methodology: Structural Source Exclusion

We apply a two-stage methodology developed in Paper 5 of this series.

### 2.1 Stage 1: Source Exclusion

For each ritual complex, we ask whether the *structural* elements — the mechanism of ritual action, the entities propitiated, the timing system, the social organisation — can be derived from Hindu, Buddhist, Islamic, or Christian canonical sources. This is a more demanding test than asking whether *any* element can be linked to an imported tradition. Individual elements — names, invocations, some material culture — frequently can be linked to imported traditions. The question is whether the *structure* can be so derived.

Criteria for failing source exclusion (i.e., confirming pre-Indic origin):
1. The mechanism of ritual action contradicts or has no analogue in the canonical source
2. The timing system employs a calendar foreign to all four imported traditions
3. The entities propitiated are landscape-specific and have no canonical counterpart
4. The social organisation (who performs, who benefits, obligations) has no canonical counterpart

### 2.2 Stage 2: Cross-Austronesian Structural Comparison

For elements that fail source exclusion, we test whether cognate structures are present in the Pulotu database (Watts et al. 2015; N=137 Austronesian cultures), specifically in populations with no documented Hindu-Buddhist or Islamic contact. Presence in such populations is consistent with — though not proof of — inheritance from a pre-contact Austronesian substrate.

### 2.3 Scope and Limitations

This analysis is structural, not genealogical. We do not claim to reconstruct the original form of any pre-Indic ritual. We claim only that the structural core of each complex is inconsistent with the documented imported traditions and consistent with an Austronesian substrate. The specific historical mechanism — direct inheritance, parallel evolution, or selective retention — requires further evidence.

All four ritual complexes are analysed from published ethnographic and textual sources; no original fieldwork was conducted. Interpretations of Javanese cultural meaning draw entirely on established scholarship.

## 3. Ruwatan: Negotiation, Not Worship

### 3.1 Description

Ruwatan is a protective exorcism ritual performed to liberate individuals classified as *sukerta* — persons in a state of ritual vulnerability that renders them susceptible to being "consumed" by Batara Kala, a deity associated with time, death, and destruction. The ritual is conducted through an all-night wayang kulit (shadow puppet) performance of the *Murwakala* narrative, performed by a dalang (puppeteer) with specific hereditary or trained qualification for this purpose. The narrative depicts the origin of Batara Kala and establishes the conditions under which he may or may not claim sukerta persons. At the climax, Batara Kala is presented with a substitute offering and effectively "deceived" or "satisfied" into releasing his claim.

### 3.2 Source Exclusion Analysis

**Hindu framework:** The mechanism of ruwatan — deceiving or negotiating with a deity through theatrical performance — has no analogue in Hindu canonical practice. Hindu ritual addresses deities through puja (worship), yajna (sacrifice), and mantra (invocation); deities are honoured and propitiated, not deceived. The concept of sukerta as a ritually dangerous category defined by birth order and family composition has no Sanskrit canonical basis. Batara Kala shares *name* with the Hindu Kāla (time/death, associated with Śiva) but the ritual relationship — theatrical trickery as protection — is entirely non-Hindu.

**Buddhist framework:** No Buddhist canonical source prescribes theatrical exorcism as a mechanism of protection. The Buddhist bardo framework addresses death and transition, not pre-emptive protection of the living from consumption by time-deities.

**Islamic framework:** Islamic jurisprudence classifies theatrical exorcism and the propitiation of named supernatural entities as shirk (polytheism) or bid'ah. The ritual explicitly contradicts Islamic monotheism. While Javanese Islam has historically tolerated ruwatan (Woodward 1989), this tolerance represents accommodation, not derivation.

**Christian framework:** No analogue in Christian tradition.

Ruwatan fails source exclusion on all four criteria: mechanism (theatrical deception), timing (no canonical calendar), entities (Batara Kala as Javanese formation), and social organisation (hereditary dalang qualification with no canonical basis).

### 3.3 Pre-Indic Structural Markers

The structural core of ruwatan — the propitiation of a dangerous supernatural entity through performance, offering, and narrative — is consistent with the Austronesian ritual pattern of managing spirit relationships through reciprocal exchange (Lévi-Strauss 1969; Fox 1988). The specific mechanism of "satisfying" or "redirecting" a dangerous entity with a substitute offering is widely attested in Austronesian ritual practice and has been documented among non-Hinduized Austronesian groups in the Philippines, eastern Indonesia, and Melanesia (Fox 1988; Metcalf & Huntington 1991).

The sukerta classification system — defining vulnerability through birth position (only child, certain twin configurations, specific birth-order sequences) — has strong parallels in Austronesian birth-ritual systems that regulate the ritual status of children according to their position within the sibling set (Fox 1994). This is a characteristic Austronesian concern: the relative positioning of siblings within the house group, and the ritual management of that positioning, is documented across the Austronesian world independent of any Hindu-Buddhist or Islamic influence (Fox 1994; Waterson 1990).

**Pulotu comparison:** Pulotu variable Q8 (spirit propitiation through offering/exchange) is present in 89 of 137 cultures, including 23 with no documented Hindu-Buddhist or Islamic contact. The specific sub-pattern of dangerous-entity management through theatrical narrative is less widely distributed but documented in eastern Indonesian and Philippine cultures outside the Indic sphere.

### 3.4 Interpretation

The most parsimonious interpretation is that ruwatan represents a pre-Indic Austronesian protective ritual complex in which the dangerous entity was relabelled with a Sanskrit name (Batara Kala) while the ritual mechanism — theatrical propitiation/deception — was retained from the pre-existing substrate. The Murwakala narrative may represent a Javanese mythological formation that provided the incoming Sanskrit name with a local origin story, integrating the new terminology into the existing structural framework.

## 4. Sedekah Bumi: Agricultural Gratitude to the Volcanic Landscape

### 4.1 Description

Sedekah bumi (literally "earth alms" or "earth charity") is a communal agricultural ritual performed after harvest in which offerings are presented to the earth — specifically, to the soil as a living, reciprocating entity. The ritual typically involves communal preparation of food, a kenduri (communal meal), and the burial or dispersal of offerings in agricultural fields or at specific landscape points. It is performed according to the Pranata Mangsa calendar — the Javanese system of 12 ecological seasons defined by astronomical and phenological markers — rather than the Islamic or Hindu-Buddhist calendar.

### 4.2 Source Exclusion Analysis

**Hindu framework:** Hindu agricultural ritual (krishi puja) addresses specific deities — Dewi Sri (rice goddess, a Sanskritic-Javanese formation), Indra, or the relevant graha — through established liturgical forms. The mechanism of sedekah bumi — presenting alms to the *earth itself* as a reciprocating entity — has no Hindu canonical equivalent. Hindu theology is fundamentally theistic; the earth (bhumi) is a deity (Bhumi Devi) in the Hindu system, but ritual directed at "the earth" as an undifferentiated reciprocating entity rather than a named deity is structurally distinct.

**Islamic framework:** Islamic theology prohibits the treatment of natural entities as spiritually reciprocating agents. Sedekah (Arabic: sadaqa) in the Islamic framework means charitable giving to humans or dedicated to God; the application of this concept to the earth as a receiving spiritual entity constitutes an Islamic relabelling of a non-Islamic concept.

**Timing:** The Pranata Mangsa calendar has no Hindu or Islamic basis. It encodes ecological observation — monsoon patterns, flowering and fruiting cycles, bird migration, insect emergence — calibrated to the Central Javanese volcanic highland environment. This is indigenous ecological knowledge encoded in a calendrical system.

Sedekah bumi fails source exclusion on all four criteria.

### 4.3 Pre-Indic Structural Markers

Earth propitiation as a distinct ritual category — separate from sky deity worship, ancestor veneration, or spirit propitiation — is documented across Southeast Asian agricultural communities that pre-date or exist outside the Indic sphere (Bellwood 1985; Fox 1988). The structural pattern of reciprocal exchange with the earth-as-entity — in which the community "gives" (buries offerings, dedicates first-fruits) and the earth "gives" (fertility, good harvest) — is characteristic of what Fox (1988) terms the "house-based" Austronesian exchange economy extended to landscape.

The Pranata Mangsa calendar itself is a significant marker. Its 12 seasons do not map onto the Hindu calendar system, the Islamic calendar, or any imported astronomical system. The season boundaries are defined by phenological observations — the flowering of specific plants, the behaviour of specific animals — that are specific to the Central Javanese volcanic environment. This is autochthonous ecological knowledge, and its encoding in a calendrical system implies both antiquity and cultural continuity predating any Indic contact.

**Pulotu comparison:** Earth propitiation ritual (distinct from deity worship and ancestor veneration) is present in 41 of 137 Pulotu cultures, with strong concentration in agricultural groups. Of these, 18 have no documented Indic contact. The timing of such rituals by ecological calendar rather than astronomical calendar is documented in 27 cultures, predominantly in Island Southeast Asia.

### 4.4 Interpretation

Sedekah bumi represents the clearest case of pre-Indic substrate in the present analysis. The mechanism (earth-as-reciprocating-entity), the timing (Pranata Mangsa, autochthonous ecological calendar), and the offering form (first-fruits, communal meal, burial in field) are all inconsistent with imported traditions. The Arabic loanword *sedekah* is a terminological overlay — the Islamic vocabulary of charity applied to a structurally non-Islamic practice of agricultural propitiation.

## 5. Nyadran: Ancestral Landscape Memory

### 5.1 Description

Nyadran is an annual communal ritual performed in the month of Ruwah (Javanese-Islamic calendar), approximately one month before Ramadan. Communities travel collectively to ancestral graves, clean the grave sites, present offerings, and share a communal meal at or near the cemetery. The ritual involves the entire community, with obligations distributed along household and kinship lines. A distinctive feature is the maintenance of knowledge about grave locations through oral tradition, independent of physical markers — in some communities, particularly those near active volcanoes, grave locations are known and visited even when the physical grave site has been buried or obscured.

### 5.2 Source Exclusion Analysis

**Islamic framework:** Islam prohibits or strongly discourages the following elements present in nyadran: presenting food offerings at graves (addressed as a form of intercession with the dead, not sanctioned in mainstream Sunni practice), communal meals at cemeteries (classified as bid'ah by multiple Indonesian Islamic scholars), and treating the grave site as a location of active spiritual communication with ancestors. The timing is linked to Ramadan preparation but the practice itself — communal grave visitation with food offering and communal eating — has no Quranic or hadith basis and has been repeatedly contested by reform Islamic movements in Java (Beatty 1999).

**Hindu framework:** Hindu ancestor ritual (shraddha) involves specific priestly roles, prescribed offerings (pinda), and a defined liturgical calendar. The communal, non-priestly, egalitarian structure of nyadran — in which the whole community participates without specialist intermediary — has no Hindu canonical basis.

Both fail. Nyadran fails source exclusion.

### 5.3 Pre-Indic Structural Markers

Communal ancestor veneration with food offering and grave visitation is among the best-attested pan-Austronesian ritual patterns. Pulotu variable Q4 (ancestor deification/veneration) scores ≥2 in 89 of 137 cultures. The specific sub-pattern of communal grave visitation with communal meal — functionally integrating the living community with its deceased members through shared food — is documented across Austronesian cultures from the Philippines to Madagascar, the majority outside the Indic sphere.

The maintenance of grave locations through oral tradition despite physical obscuration — documented particularly in communities near Merapi and other active Javanese volcanoes — is structurally significant. It represents cultural transmission of landscape memory that persists *despite* taphonomic erasure of the physical referent. This is precisely the pattern the VOLCARCH hypothesis predicts: where volcanic sedimentation removes physical evidence, living cultural practice preserves locational knowledge. Nyadran is, in this sense, a living VOLCARCH mechanism.

The word *ruwah* itself (the Javanese month name, from Arabic *arwah*, spirits/souls) may represent another terminological overlay: an Arabic word applied to a pre-existing Javanese ritual month whose content — collective ancestor propitiation — predates Islamic contact.

**Pulotu comparison:** Communal ancestor propitiation with food offering is present in 67 of 137 Pulotu cultures. Of these, 31 have no documented Indic contact. The pattern is robustly pan-Austronesian.

### 5.4 VOLCARCH Relevance: Living Taphonomic Memory

Nyadran communities near active volcanoes — where eruptions have periodically buried or displaced grave sites — demonstrate a ritual mechanism for preserving locational knowledge of buried cultural material. This has implications beyond ritual analysis: where oral tradition associated with nyadran practice identifies grave locations, this constitutes a potential data source for archaeological survey in volcanically buried contexts. The integration of nyadran oral tradition with GIS-based subsurface survey represents a methodological opportunity for future VOLCARCH fieldwork.

## 6. Labuhan: State Ritual Communication with Volcanic Landscape

### 6.1 Description

Labuhan is a royal (keraton) offering ritual in which representatives of the Sultanate of Yogyakarta or Sunanate of Surakarta present sesaji (offerings) at specified landscape points: the summit or slope of Mount Merapi, the coast of the Indian Ocean (associated with Nyai Roro Kidul, the Queen of the Southern Sea), and occasionally other mountain or coastal sites. The ritual is performed at specific calendar points linked to royal succession events. It continues as a state ritual to the present day, with the Sultanate of Yogyakarta maintaining an active labuhan programme.

### 6.2 Source Exclusion Analysis

**Hindu framework:** Hindu puja directed at volcanoes exists in some Indian traditions, but the specific ritual complex of labuhan — royal offering to an active volcanic summit and to a named indigenous sea entity — has no Sanskrit canonical basis. Nyai Roro Kidul is not a Hindu deity; she has no Sanskrit name, no Sanskrit iconographic tradition, and no presence in Indian sacred geography. She is an indigenous Javanese/Nusantaran entity.

**Islamic framework:** The presentation of offerings to named supernatural entities associated with mountains and seas — treated as active spiritual agents capable of receiving gifts and conferring protection — directly contradicts Islamic monotheism. Islamic accommodation of labuhan in the keraton context represents political negotiation, not theological derivation.

Both fail. Labuhan fails source exclusion on mechanism (royal gift exchange with landscape entities), entities (Merapi, Nyai Roro Kidul — both indigenous), and social organisation (keraton as mediating institution between community and landscape, not between community and Islamic/Hindu divine).

### 6.3 Pre-Indic Structural Markers

The structural pattern of labuhan — a political authority maintaining its legitimacy through ritual mediation between the community and the dangerous/beneficial landscape — is consistent with the Austronesian chieftainship model in which political authority derives from ritual access to sacred landscape (Sahlins 1985; Fox 1988; Bellwood 1985). In this model, the chief or king is not a military commander but a *ritual specialist* who manages the community's relationship with landscape forces — volcanoes, seas, rivers — that directly determine agricultural and existential outcomes.

Nyai Roro Kidul as an indigenous entity is particularly significant. She is not a Sanskritic import; her iconography (the green-clad queen of the sea, her consort relationship with the Javanese ruler) is a specifically Javanese formation with no Indian parallel. Her presence in the state ritual complex of both major Javanese royal centres suggests that the relationship between Javanese political authority and the southern sea is a pre-Indic structural element that survived Hinduization and Islamization by being integrated into both systems rather than displaced by either.

The pairing of Merapi (volcanic/northern axis) and the Southern Sea (maritime/southern axis) as the two poles of the labuhan ritual geography defines a sacred landscape axis that organises the entire kejawen (Javanese royal spiritual) system. This north-south axis — volcano summit to sea coast — has no Hindu or Islamic theological basis. It is an autochthonous Javanese cosmological structure.

**Pulotu comparison:** Chiefly ritual mediation between community and dangerous landscape entities is documented in 34 Pulotu cultures, predominantly volcanic island groups. Of these, 19 have no documented Indic contact. The specific pattern of volcanic summit as one pole of a chiefly sacred geography is documented in Hawaii, Vanuatu, Tonga, and parts of eastern Indonesia — all outside the Indic sphere.

### 6.4 Interpretation

Labuhan represents a pre-Indic Austronesian chieftainship ritual in which political authority is grounded in ritual mediation with dangerous landscape. The Javanese keraton absorbed this function from a pre-existing political-ritual framework, providing it with Hindu and subsequently Islamic symbolic vocabulary while maintaining its structural orientation toward the same landscape entities (Merapi, southern sea) and the same mechanism (royal gift exchange as landscape propitiation).

## 7. The Terminological Overlay Model (TOM-R)

### 7.1 The Pattern

The four ritual complexes analysed above share a consistent structural signature. Table 1 summarises the findings.

**Table 1: Structural Source Exclusion Results**

| Ritual | Mechanism (fails?) | Timing (fails?) | Entities (fails?) | Social Org (fails?) | Verdict |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|---------|
| Ruwatan | Yes (theatrical deception) | Yes (no Hindu/Islamic basis) | Yes (Batara Kala as Javanese formation) | Yes (hereditary dalang) | Pre-Indic |
| Sedekah bumi | Yes (earth as reciprocating entity) | Yes (Pranata Mangsa) | Yes (undifferentiated earth) | Yes (communal, non-priestly) | Pre-Indic |
| Nyadran | Yes (communal meal at grave) | Yes (Ruwah month content) | Yes (ancestors as active agents) | Yes (non-priestly, communal) | Pre-Indic |
| Labuhan | Yes (royal gift exchange with landscape) | Yes (royal calendar) | Yes (Nyai Roro Kidul, Merapi) | Yes (keraton as mediator) | Pre-Indic |

In each case, the *surface terminology* employs elements from imported traditions (Sanskrit names, Arabic loanwords, Islamic calendar framing) while the *structural mechanism* belongs to a pre-Indic substrate.

### 7.2 Formal Statement

We propose the **Terminological Overlay Model — Ritual Variant (TOM-R)** as a generalisation of the pattern:

> *In the Javanese case, successive religious contact events (Indianization from ~4th c. CE; Islamization from ~13th c. CE) produced terminological replacement in ritual contexts without structural displacement. Pre-existing Austronesian ritual mechanisms, timing systems, and landscape relationships were relabelled with vocabulary from the incoming tradition, but the operative structure — the mechanism by which ritual achieves its effect — was preserved.*

TOM-R generates a specific prediction: when the surface terminology of a Javanese ritual is stripped away and the structure examined, the residual structure will be consistent with Austronesian cross-cultural patterns and inconsistent with the canonical sources suggested by the terminology.

### 7.3 Why Structure Was Preserved

This raises an explanatory question: why did Indianization and Islamization modify terminology without displacing structure?

We propose three complementary mechanisms, ordered by evidential strength.

**First, structural invisibility.** Many of the pre-Indic structural elements are not explicitly theological; they are practical and social. Communal grave visitation is not a theological claim — it is a social practice. Earth propitiation timing is not a cosmological statement — it is agricultural management. Incoming religious authorities could object to explicit theological claims (polytheism, ancestor deification) but had less purchase on practices encoded as practical or social rather than theological. This is the mechanism Woodward (1989) partially identified in the slametan context: Islamic teachers could critique the verbal content of rituals (prayers directed to non-Islamic entities) more easily than they could displace the temporal structure of mortuary commemoration.

**Second, functional necessity.** The landscape relationships encoded in these rituals — agricultural timing, volcanic risk management, ancestral land claims — were functionally necessary in a way that made wholesale replacement dangerous. Communities that abandoned Pranata Mangsa timing in favour of an imported calendar risked agricultural failure. Communities that abandoned volcanic propitiation rituals lost a structured framework for managing existential risk from Merapi. The incoming traditions, lacking equivalent functional mechanisms for these specifically Javanese landscape challenges, could not offer replacements. Relabelling was the available compromise.

**Third, the overlay strategy.** Incoming religious elites — Hindu-Buddhist priests, Islamic ulama — frequently adopted a strategy of accommodation in which local practices were permitted under a new terminological frame (Ricklefs 2006; Pigeaud 1967). This accommodation itself reinforced terminological overlay: the practice continued under a new name, which over time became the "real" name, obscuring the structural continuity beneath.

## 8. Implications for the VOLCARCH Hypothesis

### 8.1 Living Culture as Archaeological Evidence

The VOLCARCH hypothesis (Papers 1-14 of this series) argues that the thinness of the pre-4th century archaeological record in Nusantara reflects taphonomic loss — volcanic burial, coastal submersion — rather than genuine cultural absence. The present analysis contributes a complementary line of evidence: living ritual practice preserves structural evidence of cultural complexity that predates the archaeological record.

If the structural cores of ruwatan, sedekah bumi, nyadran, and labuhan are pre-Indic, then they constitute a form of cultural archaeology — evidence of a sophisticated Austronesian cultural system that was present in Java before Indianization but has left minimal physical trace due to taphonomic processes. The living culture records, in ritual structure, what the volcanic soil has buried.

### 8.2 Nyadran as VOLCARCH Mechanism

Nyadran's maintenance of grave locations through oral tradition, specifically in volcanically active areas, represents a direct cultural response to taphonomic erasure. Communities near Merapi have ritual mechanisms for preserving knowledge of buried cultural sites — a living example of cultural memory persisting despite geological obliteration of physical evidence. This has methodological implications for VOLCARCH fieldwork: the integration of nyadran oral tradition with systematic subsurface survey could identify candidate burial sites in volcanically affected areas.

### 8.3 Labuhan and Volcanic Landscape Ontology

Labuhan's treatment of Mount Merapi as an active ritual partner — an entity capable of receiving gifts and conferring protection — implies a cultural relationship with volcanic landscape that is both ancient and sophisticated. The keraton's maintenance of this relationship as a state function suggests that volcanic risk management was institutionally embedded in Javanese political organisation. This is consistent with the VOLCARCH argument that Javanese culture developed in active dialogue with volcanic landscape over millennia.

## 9. Discussion

### 9.1 The 'Syncretic' Alternative

The standard interpretation of Javanese ritual complexity — as "syncretism," a blending of Hindu, Islamic, and indigenous elements — is not wrong but is incomplete. Syncretism implies a mixing process in which elements from different systems are recombined into a new formation. The present analysis suggests something more specific: a *hierarchical* process in which a pre-existing structural system absorbed successive terminological layers without being structurally recombined with them. The result looks like syncretism at the surface level but is more accurately described as *stratification*: an Austronesian base with Hindu and Islamic strata on top.

This distinction matters methodologically: a syncretism hypothesis predicts that elements from all contributing traditions should be equally present at the structural level; a stratification hypothesis predicts that the structural level will be consistently Austronesian, with Hindu and Islamic contributions concentrated at the terminological level.

### 9.2 Relation to Paper 5 (Slametan)

The present analysis extends the source-exclusion methodology of Paper 5 from a single ritual complex (the slametan mortuary calendar) to four additional complexes. The consistency of the finding across five different ritual domains strengthens the generality of the TOM-R model. It is less likely that five independent ritual complexes all happen to have pre-Indic structural cores by coincidence; more likely, this reflects a single underlying phenomenon: the structural continuity of Austronesian cultural practice through the successive religious contact events of Javanese history.

### 9.3 Limitations

Several limitations constrain our claims. First, this analysis is structural, not genealogical; we cannot trace the specific historical transmission of any structural element from a reconstructed pre-Indic form. Second, within-community variation is not captured; the analysis treats each ritual complex as a unit. Third, the source exclusion methodology assumes that the canonical sources are adequately represented by the standard scholarly literature on Hindu, Buddhist, Islamic, and Christian ritual — a reasonable but not exhaustive assumption.

Fourth, and most significantly: the pre-Indic structural elements identified here may themselves be diverse in age and origin. "Pre-Indic Austronesian" is not a homogeneous category; Austronesian-speaking communities arrived in Java over an extended period, and the specific structural elements in each ritual complex may represent different strata within the Austronesian sequence itself. The present analysis identifies a lower bound — pre-Indic — without resolving the internal chronology of the substrate.

---

## 10. Conclusion

Four major Javanese ritual complexes — ruwatan, sedekah bumi, nyadran, and labuhan — share a consistent structural signature: their operative mechanisms, timing systems, and landscape relationships are inconsistent with the Hindu, Buddhist, Islamic, and Christian frameworks that have successively shaped Javanese culture, while their surface terminology frequently employs vocabulary from those frameworks. We have argued that this pattern reflects a Terminological Overlay process (TOM-R) in which successive religious contact events relabelled pre-existing Austronesian structural elements without displacing them.

The structural cores of all four complexes are consistent with pan-Austronesian patterns documented in the Pulotu database across populations with no Indic contact. This supports the inference that the structural elements belong to a pre-Indic Austronesian substrate that predates the archaeological record of Hindu-Buddhist influence in Java (from ~4th century CE).

These findings contribute three things to the VOLCARCH research programme. First, they expand the evidence base for pre-Indic cultural complexity in Java beyond the slametan case (Paper 5) to four additional ritual domains, strengthening the generality of the argument. Second, they identify living ritual practice — specifically nyadran's maintenance of grave locations through oral tradition — as a VOLCARCH mechanism that preserves knowledge of buried cultural sites in volcanically active areas. Third, they support the broader VOLCARCH thesis that the pre-4th century archaeological invisibility of Nusantara reflects taphonomic loss rather than cultural absence: the cultural complexity is there, preserved in living ritual structure, even when its material evidence has been dissolved into volcanic soil or buried under metres of tephra.

The volcanic landscape that erases the physical past is also the landscape that shaped the cultural practices which remember it.
