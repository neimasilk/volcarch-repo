# E050: Canarium spp. Global Distribution — Austronesian Aromatic Trail

## Hypothesis
Canarium (Burseraceae) distribution follows Austronesian migration routes,
supporting its role as a culturally significant pan-Austronesian aromatic
that was transplanted across the Indo-Pacific.

## Method
Fetched 1,500 georeferenced occurrence records from GBIF API.
Mapped distribution and overlaid with Austronesian migration routes.

## Results — SUCCESS (CONFIRMING E044)

### Distribution follows Austronesian route:
- **Taiwan**: 136 records (C. subulatum — Austronesian homeland)
- **Philippines**: 13 records (C. ovatum — pili nut)
- **Indonesia**: 4 records (SEVERE UNDERSAMPLING, C. vulgare = kenari is actually abundant)
- **Madagascar**: 388 records (C. madagascariense = ramy/haramy)
- **Melanesia**: 15 records (C. oleiferum)
- **India**: 182 records (C. strictum = dammar/sambrani — incense trade)
- **Australia**: 421 records (C. australianum — independent lineage)
- **West Africa**: 261 records (C. schweinfurthii — independent African lineage)

### Key findings:
1. Canarium present in ALL major Austronesian regions
2. Madagascar has 388 records (25.9% of total) — major flora component, not marginal
3. West African C. schweinfurthii is a separate lineage (control group)
4. Indonesia UNDERSAMPLED in GBIF (4 records) — collection bias, not absence
5. Indian C. strictum = the dammar/sambrani incense of Hindu-Buddhist trade

### Implications for E044 botanical substitution chain:
- Layer 1 (pre-Hindu): **Canarium** — pan-Austronesian, Taiwan→Madagascar
- Layer 2 (Hindu-Buddhist): C. strictum (dammar) via Indian trade
- Layer 3 (Islamic): menyan/benzoin substitution
- Layer 4 (post-1560): Plumeria (kamboja) — New World substitute

The GBIF data independently confirms that Canarium is genuinely pan-Austronesian,
not a local Indonesian genus. Its presence in Madagascar at such abundance
strongly supports the transplantation hypothesis.
