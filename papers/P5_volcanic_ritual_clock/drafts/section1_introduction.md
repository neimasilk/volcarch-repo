# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The problem

Death rituals are among the most conservative elements of any cultural repertoire. Mortuary practices persist across religious conversions, political upheavals, and economic transformations with a tenacity that other social institutions rarely match (Bloch and Parry 1982; Metcalf and Huntington 1991). Yet the material correlates of death — bodies, offerings, grave goods — are subject to taphonomic destruction, and this destruction proceeds at rates that vary dramatically with local geology. What has received little attention is the possibility that mortuary timing — the specific intervals at which commemorative rites are performed — may encode empirical observation of a physical process: the decomposition of the human body in local soil.

This paper addresses that possibility in the volcanic plains of Java. In volcanic environments, two taphonomic processes converge. The first is chemical: Javanese Andosols are characteristically acidic (pH 4.5-5.5), a regime in which the mineral component of bone, hydroxyapatite, undergoes active dissolution. Experimental studies demonstrate measurable bone degradation within weeks at pH values below 5.0 (Oghenemavwe et al. 2022; Hedges and Millard 1995), and forensic case reports from acidic environments document near-complete mineral loss in remains buried for only a few years (Kemp 2016). Tropical temperatures (26-28 degrees Celsius) and high moisture compress the trajectory from intact burial to physical disappearance still further.

The second process is depositional. Java's volcanic arc produces tephra at rates that translate to sedimentation of approximately 3.6 millimetres per year in the alluvial plains surrounding major stratovolcanoes (Amien 2026a, 2026b). Within a millennium, sites are buried nearly four metres deep — beyond the reach of surface survey. These two processes operate at different timescales but share a common driver: volcanism. This convergence raises a question not previously posed in the literature: could the timing of mortuary ritual in volcanic landscapes reflect generations of empirical observation of how bodies decompose in that specific soil?

## 1.2 The slametan cycle

The Javanese *slametan* is a communal ritual meal marking transitions of many kinds; its mortuary variant follows a structured sequence of commemorations that is remarkably consistent across regions, social classes, and religious affiliations (Geertz 1960; Beatty 1999). The standard sequence runs: *nelung dina* (third day), *mitung dina* (seventh day), *matang puluh* (fortieth day), *nyatus* (hundredth day), *mendhak sepisan* (first anniversary), *mendhak pindo* (second anniversary), and *nyewu* (thousandth day). Each successive ceremony is larger than the last, building toward *nyewu* as the culmination, after which no further commemorations are held (Geertz 1960: ch. 6; Koentjaraningrat 1994; Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020).

What makes this sequence distinctive is the explicit connection drawn by Javanese tradition between the timing of each ceremony and the physical state of the body. This is not an inference by the present authors; it is stated in Javanese sources themselves. At *matang puluh* (forty days), the ceremony 'perfects' (*menyempurnakan*) specific bodily components: 'blood, flesh, marrow, innards, nails, hair, bones, and muscle' — a systematic inventory of tissues, not a vague metaphor (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 437). At *mendhak pindo* (approximately two years), the tradition holds that 'the body has almost dissolved, only bones remain' (*jenazah sudah hampir luluh, tinggal tulang saja*), a claim independently documented in two separate sources (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 439; Sholihah et al. 2023: 370). At *nyewu*, Geertz recorded that the body is believed to have 'completely decayed to dust' (1960: ch. 6); Javanese esoteric tradition states that 'from 1000 days the deceased enters the eternal dimension' (Ki Sabdalangit, cited in Suwito et al. 2015).

The soul's progressive journey mirrors the body's dissolution. At three days, the spirit remains in the house; at seven, it begins to depart; at forty, it leaves the immediate surroundings; at one thousand, it 'will no longer return to the family' (Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim 2020: 440). The critical observation is that the ritual's endpoint is defined by a physical criterion, not a theological one. *Nyewu* is not the day on which a doctrine declares the soul complete; it is the day on which, in volcanic soil, the body can no longer be found.

## 1.3 Prior work

Robert Hertz (1907/1960) proposed that in societies practising secondary burial, the period between death and final ceremony corresponds to the time required for the body to reach the 'dry' state. The liminal status of the corpse — neither fully present nor fully gone — mirrors the liminal status of the soul, which cannot join the ancestors until the body has completed its transformation. Bloch and Parry (1982) extended this analysis cross-culturally, while Metcalf and Huntington (1991) documented its specific Austronesian manifestation: secondary burial and multi-stage mortuary ritual pervasive from Borneo to Madagascar, constituting a pan-Austronesian mortuary complex.

Within Javanese studies, Beatty (1999) offered a multivocal analysis of the slametan; Woodward (1989) situated it within the tension between Islamic orthodoxy and Javanese practice. Hendrajaya and Almu'tasim (2020) provided the most detailed published account of body-state beliefs at each interval, while Aizid (2015: 149) stated explicitly that the death slametan 'existed before Hinduism-Buddhism came to Indonesia', with successive religious waves modifying only the verbal content while leaving timing and structure intact.

What is absent from this literature is any attempt to test the empirical accuracy of the tradition's claims about decomposition. Hertz theorised that decomposition and ritual are linked; Javanese sources assert specific timings for specific bodily transformations; forensic taphonomy provides quantitative data on decomposition in various soils. No previous study has brought these three bodies of knowledge into contact to ask: are the Javanese claims about the body at forty days, at two years, at one thousand days, consistent with what forensic science predicts for burial in acidic volcanic soil?

## 1.4 This paper's contribution

We propose the 'Volcanic Ritual Clock' hypothesis: that the slametan mortuary sequence preserves empirically calibrated intervals corresponding to the observable stages of human decomposition in Andosol, reflecting generations of accumulated observation in volcanic landscapes. We do not claim that the slametan is 'nothing but' a decomposition calendar — its social, emotional, and cosmological dimensions are real and irreducible. We claim that the physical process of decomposition provided the empirical scaffold upon which those richer meanings were built, and that this scaffold is still legible in the tradition's own language about the body.

The argument draws on three independent lines of evidence. The first is epigraphic: analysis of the DHARMA corpus of 268 Nusantaran inscriptions reveals a persistent stratum of pre-Indic cosmological vocabulary, most notably *hyang* (PMP \**qiang*, 'spirit'), appearing in 43 per cent of inscriptions across Old Javanese, Old Sundanese, and Old Malay texts. The slametan does not appear in the epigraphic record — as expected for an oral tradition — but the cosmological substrate to which it belongs is demonstrably older than the Indianisation of Java. The second is comparative: the Pulotu database (Watts et al. 2015) reveals that at least 30 of 137 Austronesian cultures share a 'full mortuary package' of deified ancestors, active ancestral spirits, and post-death ritual efficacy — found in populations separated by thousands of kilometres, from Toraja to Merina to Ifugao — supporting common Austronesian origin. The *structure* of the mortuary complex is pan-Austronesian; the *specific intervals* differ by region, and we propose this variation reflects local environmental calibration. The third is taphonomic: drawing on forensic decomposition studies and experimental work on bone dissolution in acidic soil, we construct a predicted timeline for burial in tropical Andosol and compare it against the slametan intervals and the tradition's own body-state claims. The correspondence, as we demonstrate in Section 5, is systematic.

These lines of evidence converge on a synthesis we frame as the Hypothetical Taphonomic Overlay Model (H-TOM), which identifies two timescales linked by a single volcanic driver. At the ritual timescale — one thousand days — acidic volcanic soil reduces a buried body to physical invisibility, and the slametan marks the completion of this process. At the geological timescale — one thousand years — volcanic sedimentation buries the communities' archaeological sites beneath metres of tephra. The environment that calibrates the ritual clock also destroys the material evidence of the people who kept it. The living culture records, in ritual, the very process that erases its own material past.
